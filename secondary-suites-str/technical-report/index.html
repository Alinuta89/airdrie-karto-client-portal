1. Executive Summary

This report responds to the City of Airdrie's request for a jurisdictional scan and policy analysis of secondary-suite density controls and short-term rental (STR) regulation, set against the City's active Housing Accelerator Fund (HAF) Contribution Agreement, the recently adopted accessory-suite reforms in Bylaws B-30/2024 and B-06/2025, and the open public consultation on a four-homes-per-lot regulation closing 2026-05-25.

Bottom line. Geographic concentration caps on secondary suites are legally defensible in Alberta where calibrated to an evidentiary record, but the federal HAF risk profile is materially variable. The legal anchor case is now Red Deer, whose layered density mechanism — a hard 15% per-neighbourhood numerical cap on House Suites + Backyard Suites under Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 §4.50.5, paired with parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria — pre-dated and survived the Canada Mortgage and Housing Corporation (CMHC) HAF-2 signing. What cost Red Deer its $12M HAF agreement, effective January 16, 2026, was a separate July 2025 council vote refusing to adopt four-units-as-of-right citywide, not the existence of the cap. Calgary's April 2026 repeal-of-blanket-rezoning-with-simultaneous-suites-amendment manoeuvre is the documented conditional Canadian path for retreating from contested density rezoning while preserving HAF compliance to date; CMHC reaffirmed Calgary's third $64.7M installment conditional on a four-units-as-of-right replacement instrument being in force by 2026-10-27.

Across the 20 municipalities scanned, no large urban comparator and no mid-sized Alberta peer except Red Deer operates a numerical concentration cap on suites. Cochrane removed its 10% neighbourhood cap in February 2020 and saw secondary-suite applications grow 232% by 2024 without documented rollback pressure. Edmonton's first-year evidence under Zoning Bylaw 20001 — 122 of 242 eight-unit-eligible permits (50.4%) opted for maximum density — is the strongest empirical refutation available of the "everyone-builds-the-max" objection routinely raised against as-of-right enablement. The dominant 2024–2026 trajectory across BC, Ontario, and Saskatchewan strategic-context jurisdictions is the opposite of concentration capping: provincial density floors (BC Bill 44, ON Bill 23), citywide pre-zoning, parcel-level form-based standards, principal-residence anchors on STRs, and outcome reporting against quantified targets.

The recommended package for Airdrie is HAF-safe by construction. It comprises (a) a registration and inventory regime for accessory suites paired with parcel-level design, parking, and lot-coverage standards; (b) a first-time STR bylaw modelled on Rocky View County Bylaw C-8640-2025 (primary-residence anchor with three-document verification, 1-year → 3-year → 5-year tiered permit ladder, mandatory operations plan); (c) a vacancy-triggered moratorium clause on new non-primary STR licences, modelled on Calgary's 2.5% trigger; and (d) explicit prohibition of STR operation in a secondary suite where the suite is not the host's principal residence. Each element operates on the use of dwelling units rather than on their existence; none constrains the supply targets to which Airdrie committed under HAF. This recommendation is conditional on a 12-month Airdrie-specific measurement program (complaint volume, parking saturation, listing count, rental-stock conversion) that calibrates the package to local conditions, since the dossier was produced without primary Airdrie operational data.

The package preserves Airdrie's HAF compliance, defends against the four primary legal exposures identified in this report (reasonableness review, Bell v. The Queen user-versus-use doctrine, Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms s. 15 adverse-effect risk based on analogous authority, and Alberta Human Rights Act s. 5 family-status / source-of-income / disability exposure), and substitutes evidence-based reversibility for the political reversibility a cap would purport to provide.

2. Methodology

2.1 Research approach

The research was structured as a six-stream parallel scan, each stream prepared by a Senior Policy Researcher with a defined jurisdictional and analytical scope. Streams were:

  • Stream A — Alberta regulatory framework (Municipal Government Act (MGA) authority, existing bylaws, HAF compatibility, Bill 18).
  • Stream B — Large urban comparators (Calgary, Edmonton, Vancouver, Toronto).
  • Stream C — Mid-sized Alberta peers (Red Deer, Lethbridge, Medicine Hat, St. Albert, Cochrane, Okotoks).
  • Stream D — Strategic-context jurisdictions (Victoria, Kelowna, Kitchener, Ottawa, Surrey, Mississauga, Saskatoon).
  • Stream E — STR regulatory frameworks (cross-jurisdictional).
  • Stream F — Legal defensibility (national legal analysis, HAF risk).

All six streams read a common Concept Brief and Political Landscape document before drafting. A Phase 2 Quality Gate followed Phase 1 stream drafting: three Red Team perspectives (skeptical municipal advisor, property-management/industry witness, housing-equity / anti-displacement advocate) reviewed the integrated package; Senior Policy Researchers revised in response; the Citation Verifier conducted a full primary-source verification pass; and a Stress Tester returned a CONDITIONAL PASS verdict authorising Phase 3 deliverable launch.

2.2 Jurisdictional selection rationale

Twenty municipalities were selected across four provinces. The selection logic:

  • Calgary, Edmonton, Vancouver, Toronto — Canada's largest urban centres; each a major HAF signatory or operating under provincial density mandates; the policy benchmarks council members are most likely to be asked about.
  • Red Deer, Lethbridge, Medicine Hat, St. Albert, Cochrane, Okotoks — closest peer municipalities by Alberta provincial-context, MGA framework, and population scale; the comparator set most directly relevant to defensibility before Airdrie's specific audience.
  • Victoria, Kelowna, Kitchener, Ottawa, Surrey, Mississauga, Saskatoon — strategic-context jurisdictions chosen for documented leading-practice instruments (citywide pre-zoning, principal-residence STR regimes, tiered permit ladders, outcome reporting).
  • Rocky View County — added as Airdrie's immediate adjacent jurisdiction following the verification that Bylaw C-8640-2025 was adopted on July 8, 2025.

2.3 Search strategy

Each stream worked from a combination of (a) primary municipal sources (council minutes, bylaw text, official permit pages, news releases); (b) provincial and federal sources (statutes, regulations, ministerial fact sheets, CMHC press releases); (c) Canadian secondary sources (CBC News, Globalnews.ca, LiveWire Calgary, Red Deer Express, Storeys, Taproot Edmonton, DiscoverAirdrie, OkotoksOnline, Lethbridge Herald, St. Albert Gazette); and (d) legal-academic literature (Ontario Human Rights Commission inquiry reports, ABLawg, BLG, Gowling WLG, Reynolds Mirth Richards & Farmer LLP, University of Alberta Urban Environmental Observatory). Each citation in this report carries an "accessed" date matching the date of stream drafting (2026-05-08) or, where verified during the May 11 verification pass, that later date.

2.4 Verification approach

The Citation Verifier conducted a full pass on 2026-05-11, distinguishing primary-source verification (direct retrieval of CMHC press releases, Toronto Auditor General audit, the Provincial Priorities Regulation via CanLII, City of Red Deer official permit pages, and LiveWire Calgary coverage of the CMHC Calgary letter) from corroboration via two or more independent secondary sources (used where primary text was not retrievable in this environment). Three sources returned HTTP 403 errors during verification and are flagged in the references with the note "[secondary source, content captured via search summary; original article not directly retrievable at verification date: 2026-05-11]" per Citation Verifier instruction.

The verification record establishes HIGH confidence for the Alberta regulatory framework, large urban comparators, mid-sized Alberta peers, and legal-defensibility streams; MEDIUM-HIGH for the strategic-context and STR-frameworks streams. No new factual errors were discovered during verification that were not already flagged in the red team reports.

2.5 Limitations

The research has four acknowledged limitations.

First, the dossier was produced without renter-side consultation. Red Team 3 (housing equity advocate) flagged this in its review. The equity-analytic content has been built into Streams E and F, but the underlying consultation gap could not be closed within the research window. The integration team has accepted that renter consultation — including engagement with Community Links, Airdrie Housing Limited, and Calgary-CMA newcomer service providers — is a precondition for any final bylaw drafting that touches the cap-family mechanism category, not a post-hoc consultation. The recommended HAF-safe baseline package in §6.7 has a lower engagement bar because the package expands rather than constrains supply, but the renter consultation gap remains a methodological limitation of this report and should be addressed in the Phase 2 stress test if Airdrie chooses to pursue a cap-family mechanism.

Second, the dossier does not address Indigenous housing rights or newcomer household vulnerability as distinct equity considerations. This gap is acknowledged at §8.3; the appropriate disposition is to flag it on the record, name engagement as a precondition for any cap-family mechanism, and proceed with the HAF-safe baseline package on the understanding that it expands rather than constrains the supply most relevant to Indigenous and newcomer households.

Third, Airdrie-specific local-harm measurement is absent. No public source surveyed during this scan reports Airdrie's STR listing count, parking-saturation observations, suite-related complaint volume, fiscal model, or rental-stock conversion baseline. The recommendation in §8 is framed as conditional on a 12-month Airdrie-specific measurement program; this report does not attempt to substitute for the local data the program would generate.

Fourth, the Provincial Priorities Regulation (Alta. Reg. 21/2025) verbatim text was confirmed through CanLII and Alberta Open Government, but the Red Deer Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 §4.50.5 verbatim wording was sourced through two official City of Red Deer permit pages quoting the operative rule in plain language and through Red Deer Express coverage of council clarifications. The Citation Verifier treated this as sufficient authority for the comparative table; direct PDF extraction of the bylaw text remains a Phase 2 belt-and-braces step.

3.1 Red Deer's empirical calibration — directly relevant to §6 defensibility

Red Deer's 15% threshold was not set arbitrarily. Empirical work at adoption found that, of 61 Red Deer neighbourhoods studied, 9 exceeded 10% suite-to-single-detached-dwelling share, and 2 exceeded 15% — South Hill and West Park east (Red Deer Express, n.d.). The cap was set at the level the existing distribution showed to be the practical outer envelope of suite concentration in the city. The 10% enhanced ceiling in the five heritage neighbourhoods (Parkvale, Woodlea, Waskasoo, Riverside Meadows, Westpark) aligns with the lower decile of the same empirical distribution. This calibration is directly material to legal defensibility under the Vavilov "record-as-a-whole" reasonableness standard and is one of the strongest features of Red Deer's regulatory architecture — independent of the HAF outcome, which turned on a separate council vote (City of Red Deer, n.d.-a, n.d.-b; Red Deer Express, n.d.).

3.2 Cochrane's cap-removal — the cleanest Alberta before/after evidence

Cochrane previously operated a 10% neighbourhood limit on secondary suites and removed it on February 10, 2020, replacing it with a "street-scale" cumulative-impact test that considers parking congestion, existing utility-services capacity, and the presence of other home-based businesses on the street (Boon, 2020). Secondary-suite applications grew 232% in 2024 (95 applications, 39.3% of overall development application growth) (Town of Cochrane, 2025). This growth correlates with Cochrane's broader population expansion toward 40,000 and the federal/provincial housing-supply policy environment and cannot be attributed solely to the cap removal; the methodologically conservative reading is that the cap removal did not produce documented neighbourhood-character collapse, complaint spikes, or council rollback motions in the four years following. This is directly relevant evidence for the council audience.

4. Key Findings

4.1 Caps are uncommon among large urban comparators

None of Calgary, Edmonton, Vancouver, Toronto, Kelowna, Surrey, Ottawa, Mississauga, Victoria, Kitchener, or Saskatoon operates a numerical or geographic concentration cap on secondary suites. Where caps existed (Surrey pre-2018; Ottawa pre-2017), they were progressively removed and replaced with form-based standards. The 2024–2026 trajectory across BC, Ontario, and Saskatchewan is the opposite of concentration capping: provincial floors, citywide pre-zoning, and form regulation at the parcel level (Stream B; Stream D).

4.2 Red Deer is the only Alberta peer with a numerical neighbourhood cap; the mechanism is layered

Red Deer's Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 §4.50.5 is the cleanest Alberta example of a layered density mechanism: a hard 15% per-neighbourhood numerical cap on House Suites + Backyard Suites combined (10% enhanced cap in five named heritage neighbourhoods), enforced at application intake via the City's Web Map tool, with parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria layered on top — concentration tests at 100 metres / block / street / row; design and accessibility (corner site OR lane access OR multi-entrance street); and on-street parking availability. The cap is removed from the variance discretion of the Development Authority. Red Deer is therefore properly characterised as hard cap + criteria layered, not either/or (City of Red Deer, n.d.-a; City of Red Deer, n.d.-b; Red Deer Express, n.d.).

The empirical calibration to the observed 9-of-61 / 2-of-61 distribution is the feature most directly material to legal defensibility (§6). Red Deer is therefore properly understood as both (a) the cleanest Alberta example of an evidence-calibrated cap design, and (b) the cautionary federal-funding case — but the two characterisations attach to different regulatory decisions: the cap pre-existed the HAF cancellation; the cancellation followed the separate July 2025 refusal of four-units-as-of-right.

4.3 Cap removal correlates with supply growth without documented neighbourhood-character collapse

The Cochrane evidence (cap removed February 2020; 232% application growth by 2024 with no documented complaint spike or council rollback) is the cleanest Canadian before/after data set in the scan. The Edmonton evidence (Zoning Bylaw 20001 permitting up to 8 units; 50.4% of eligible permits opting for maximum density in year one) is the strongest empirical refutation available of the "everyone-builds-the-max" objection that is routinely raised against as-of-right enablement (Town of Cochrane, 2025; Storeys, 2025; Taproot Edmonton, 2025).

4.4 All defensible alternatives are HAF-safe

The legally cleanest density-management mechanisms — registration regimes paired with form-based standards, parcel-level parking and lot-coverage rules, owner-occupancy requirements applied to STR licensing rather than to dwelling-unit existence, vacancy-triggered moratoria on non-primary STR licences, and parcel-level discretionary proximity considerations applied at the development-permit stage (Tier 4 in the HAF taxonomy at §6.4) — are also the mechanisms that do not put HAF tranches three and four at risk. The STR toolkit specifically operates on the use of dwelling units, not on their existence, and is therefore HAF-neutral by construction (Stream E §0; Stream F §5).

4.5 Equity and displacement risks are the underweighted story

The first-order equity harm of a concentration cap is operational, not doctrinal: enforcement of a cap bylaw against non-compliant suites removes units from the housing stock, and in Airdrie's constrained rental market displaced suite tenants do not return to the regulated market on the same terms. The displaced renter is typically lower-income or elderly. The Charter s. 15 adverse-effect exposure and the Alberta Human Rights Act (AHRA) s. 5 exposure on family-status, source-of-income, and disability grounds are the doctrinal layer that compounds — but does not replace — the operational displacement harm (Stream F §§2.0–2.4).

This is the finding that earlier drafts of the streams understated and that the Stress Test and Red Team 3 review insisted on elevating. It should be held by Council before the doctrinal analysis begins and is reflected in the §6.7 recommended package, which substitutes evidence-based reversibility for the political reversibility a cap purports to provide.

5. STR vs Secondary Suite Summary

5.1 Regulatory intent diverges, but the instruments meet at one parcel

A secondary suite is a land-use classification — it answers "is this a permitted residential dwelling unit on this parcel?" An STR is a use-of-dwelling classification — it answers "for what duration and to whom is this dwelling unit being offered?" The two instruments collide at one parcel: a secondary suite can be operated as an STR. The KT-018 research question turns on which lever — concentration caps on suites or STR controls — is the more effective management tool for the neighbourhood-impact concerns (parking, noise, transient occupancy, rental-stock erosion) that overlap between the two.

5.2 Three jurisdictional answers to "can a suite be an STR?"

  • Permitted only when the suite is the host's principal residence — Toronto, Halifax, Ottawa, Vancouver, BC province-wide via Bill 35, Rocky View County under Bylaw C-8640-2025. The host's principal residence must be on the property; the suite (or the principal unit) can be the STR, but not as a separate listing.
  • Permitted under a separate non-primary licence — Calgary's two-tier model, with a higher fee and a 2.5% PBR vacancy moratorium trigger.
  • Prohibited outright in secondary suites — View Royal BC ("secondary suites are not permitted to be non-residential commercial overnight accommodation, including vacation rentals, Airbnb, etc., regardless of length of stay"); Sunshine Coast Regional District.

Streams should treat "secondary suite operated as STR" as a third regulatory category, not as either a suite question or an STR question alone (Concept Brief §2; Stream E §1.3, §7).

5.3 Rocky View C-8640-2025 is the closest-to-Airdrie regulatory analogue

Rocky View County Council adopted Bylaw C-8640-2025 on July 8, 2025, establishing a primary-residence-required, complaint-tracked, tiered-permit framework for vacation rentals: three-document verification (land title + government ID + utility records), 1-year → 3-year → 5-year permit ladder, maximum-occupancy cap (two adults per designated sleeping area, with safety standards including egress), mandatory noise mitigation and site-management plans, HOA/condo overlay, and on-parcel-only guest parking (Rocky View County, 2025a; Rocky View County, 2025b). The bylaw was informed by 448 survey responses and 2,226 comments received in fall 2024 public engagement.

For Airdrie, Rocky View is structurally the closest regulatory analogue identified in this research: a rural/suburban county adjacent to Calgary making its first STR move, with a near-identical demographic and political profile. The County's bylaw is the only Alberta-region STR regime in 2026 that combines (a) primary-residence anchor with three-document verification, (b) tiered tenure-extending permit ladder, (c) explicit operations-plan requirement, and (d) HOA/condo backstop. Airdrie's STR greenfield window can absorb most of the C-8640-2025 framework with minimal adaptation, producing immediate intermunicipal-consistency benefit at the Airdrie / Rocky View boundary.

Track-record caveat. Bylaw C-8640-2025 has been in force less than one year as of this report; no published outcome data, no compliance metric, and no judicial test are yet available. The recommendation in §6.7 treats Rocky View as a template but the implementation specification carries a one-line caveat on the under-one-year track record per Stress Test condition R5.

5.4 STR controls are HAF-safe density levers (Stream A / Stream E integration)

Of the four mechanisms Stream A identifies as both density-controlling and HAF-safe, two sit squarely inside the STR toolkit: an STR business-licence with a principal-residence rule, and an STR-prohibition-in-suites clause. The principal-residence requirement does not reduce the count of legal dwelling units on any parcel; an STR-in-suites prohibition does not prevent a suite from being built or rented long-term — it just keeps the unit in the long-term rental tier. CMHC's HAF reporting framework counts units created and zoning reforms enacted; it does not measure or penalise STR controls that protect those units' housing use. This reframes the STR discussion from "regulatory greenfield Airdrie should fill" to "a strategically valuable density-management lever Airdrie has not yet activated" (Stream E §0).

5.5 The BC transplant caveat is non-negotiable

Vancouver's and Surrey's most aggressive STR tools rest on BC Bill 35 (province-wide principal-residence requirement, $50,000 maximum bylaw-offence fines, provincial registry, platform data-sharing obligations) and BC Bill 44 (citywide SSMUH compliance). Alberta has no equivalent legislation. Recommending "Vancouver's approach" or "Surrey's approach" to Airdrie without naming the BC provincial backbone produces visible policy with invisible enforcement — residents see the rule, operators see the gap, complaints land at council without administrative tools to respond. Airdrie should draw STR enforcement design from Rocky View County, Ottawa, Mississauga, or Saskatoon — all of which operate within authorities Airdrie itself possesses (Stream D §9.7). Surrey's cap-removal narrative is transferable; Surrey's enforcement architecture is not.

6. Policy Analysis

6.1 Standard of review for municipal bylaws

A Land Use Bylaw amendment imposing a concentration cap is reviewable on a reasonableness standard under the Vavilov framework as applied to subordinate legislation. Catalyst Paper Corp. v. North Cowichan (District), 2012 SCC 2, and Canada (Minister of Citizenship and Immigration) v. Vavilov, 2019 SCC 65, operate together: a bylaw is unreasonable only where "no reasonable body informed by these factors could have taken [the decision]" (Stewart McDannold Stuart, 2025; Allard School of Law, 2020).

The doctrine sounds like deep deference and at the outcome level largely is — courts will not redesign zoning. But two pressure points matter for caps:

  • Vavilov requires the reviewing court to look at the record as a whole to verify the bylaw's reasoning. A concentration cap with no evidentiary record explaining why this percentage or why this distance is more vulnerable than a cap supported by parking studies, nuisance-complaint data, or infrastructure-capacity analysis (Lerners LLP, n.d.). Red Deer's calibration of its 15% threshold to the observed 9-of-61 / 2-of-61 distribution is exactly the kind of record that materially strengthens defensibility on this vector (§3.1).
  • Bylaws can also be set aside for bad faith / improper purpose. In Hauff v. Vancouver (City), the BC Court of Appeal quashed a downzoning bylaw enacted in bad faith and for an improper purpose (BLG, 2020). A cap that on the deliberative record was driven by hostility to renters or specific demographic groups — rather than by parking, infrastructure, or design considerations — would face this exposure.

6.2 The user-vs-use doctrine — the strongest doctrinal limit on caps

Canadian zoning law operates under the Bell v. The Queen doctrine, often called the "user vs. use" principle: zoning powers do not permit a municipality to define a use of land by reference to the personal characteristics of the user (Reynolds Mirth Richards & Farmer LLP, n.d.). A bylaw that capped suites by reference to who lives in them would be ultra vires. A bylaw that capped suites neutrally — "no more than X% of parcels on any block" — survives the user/use test on its face but can still be challenged on adverse-effect grounds and on reasonableness (§§6.3, 6.1).

6.3 Charter s. 15 and AHRA s. 5 — running on parallel tracks

The leading Canadian authority on Charter-based challenges to neighbourhood-level housing-distribution rules is Alcoholism Foundation of Manitoba v. Winnipeg (City). The Manitoba Court of Appeal held that the City of Winnipeg's zoning bylaw breached s. 15 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms because it restricted group homes for older persons, persons with disabilities, persons recovering from addictions, and discharged inmates to a limited number of zones and required minimum separation distances between such homes (Ontario Human Rights Commission, n.d.-a; Reynolds Mirth Richards & Farmer LLP, n.d.). The Court found it unacceptable "to prohibit a use of land with reference to the attributes of those who may use it, at least where the attributes are those which distinguish members of a disadvantaged group and where there is no evidence to show that such a prohibition is one which can be demonstrably justified as reasonable."

A concentration cap on secondary suites is high-risk on this front because secondary suites are disproportionately occupied by groups that overlap with Charter-protected and Code-protected characteristics: low-income tenants, single parents, newcomers, students, persons with disabilities living in accessible units, and seniors aging in place. The Ontario Human Rights Commission has stated unambiguously that "minimum separation distances are about regulating people, and often flow from stereotypes associated with renters" (Ontario Human Rights Commission, n.d.-b). The Charter pathway in this report is therefore characterised as plausible Charter exposure based on analogous authority — no Canadian court has yet directly adjudicated a suite concentration cap, and the doctrinal record rests on the Alcoholism Foundation line and OHRC inquiry record.

The AHRA pathway runs alongside the Charter pathway, not behind it. The Alberta Human Rights Act, R.S.A. 2000, c. A-25.5, s. 5, prohibits discrimination "in the provision of accommodation, services or facilities customarily available to the public" on grounds including family status, source of income (under the tenancy and housing provisions), and physical and mental disability. A concentration cap that demonstrably reduces the supply of secondary suites — disproportionately the housing form for single-parent and multigenerational households, lower-income tenants, and persons relying on accessible suites — faces AHRA challenge independent of the Charter pathway. The AHRA route does not require state action in the Charter sense; the Commission's investigative function is less deferential than judicial reasonableness review (University of Alberta Urban Environmental Observatory, n.d.).

6.4 The four-tier HAF taxonomy

Earlier framings characterised "concentration caps" as a single HAF risk category. That framing was wrong and is corrected here. The Red Deer cancellation, the Calgary letter, and the available CMHC enforcement record support a four-tier taxonomy, not a binary cap/no-cap distinction. Each tier carries a different HAF exposure profile.

Tier Mechanism HAF status on the available evidence Controlling precedent
1 Refusal to adopt a mandated expansion commitment (e.g., refusing four-units-as-of-right) Termination trigger — confirmed Red Deer (cancellation effective 2026-01-16)
2 Net-new cap or barrier adopted post-HAF-signing that materially constrains the agreed supply targets Instalment-pause / conditioning trigger — confirmed; potentially termination if uncured Calgary (CMHC letter 2026-02-10; conditional third $64.7M instalment with 2026-10-27 replacement-zoning deadline)
3 Cap that pre-dated the HAF agreement and remained in force at signing Not inherently a trigger — CMHC signed Red Deer's HAF-2 with the 15% neighbourhood numerical cap and the parcel-level Location Criteria in Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 already on the books Red Deer pre-HAF-2 posture
4 Discretionary proximity considerations applied post-HAF at the development-permit stage that do not constrain supply targets Not a trigger on available evidence — Red Deer's parcel-level Location Criteria operated through the discretionary-permit stage and were not flagged by CMHC Red Deer's parcel-level Location Criteria

Two precise framings the council reader should hold. First, CMHC cancelled Red Deer's HAF-2 agreement on January 16, 2026 because Red Deer refused to adopt four-units-as-of-right — not because Red Deer had a concentration cap. The cap and the Location Criteria pre-dated and coexisted with the HAF-2 agreement (CMHC, 2026a). Second, CMHC's February 10, 2026 letter to Calgary on "additional approval processes and other barriers" establishes the controlling precedent for Tier 2 risk; the functional-equivalence standard in that letter applies to all housing forms under the HAF Contribution Agreement, not specifically to secondary suites (LiveWire Calgary, 2026a).

For Airdrie specifically, Tier 3 does not apply because Airdrie did not have a cap at HAF signing; Tier 1 is the do-not zone; Tier 2 is the relevant tier for any cap-family mechanism Airdrie might adopt now; Tier 4 (parcel-level discretionary proximity considerations applied at the development-permit stage, with a documented evidentiary record on what the proximity considerations are intended to address) is the lowest-risk cap-adjacent surface and the closest fit to the §6.7 "defensible alternatives" recommendation.

6.5 The Provincial Priorities Act two-key amendment pathway

Airdrie's $24.8M HAF Contribution Agreement, signed in 2024, pre-dates the Provincial Priorities Act (Bill 18), in force April 1, 2025, and is therefore grandfathered. But s. 2(1) of the Act provides that "no provincial entity, by itself or with any other entity, may enter into, amend, extend or renew an intergovernmental agreement without obtaining prior approval in accordance with a process established in the regulations" (Provincial Priorities Act, S.A. 2024, c. P-35.5).

The plain reading of Alta. Reg. 21/2025 (the Provincial Priorities Regulation) and ministerial guidance is that the $250,000 housing exemption attaches to the agreement value, not the amendment value (Alberta Municipalities, 2025; Government of Alberta, n.d.-a). A $24.8M HAF agreement is far above the $250K housing threshold and the $5M Cabinet threshold; any substantive amendment of a $24.8M agreement is, on the plain reading, an amendment of an above-threshold agreement and therefore subject to Cabinet approval — irrespective of how large the amendment itself is.

The implication is a two-key approval pathway for any negotiated retreat from Airdrie's HAF commitments: CMHC consent on the federal side, against the backdrop of the Calgary functional-equivalence standard and the Red Deer termination precedent; and Alberta Cabinet approval under the PPA on the provincial side, given the >$5M agreement value. Either side can independently block the change. Given the Premier's and Minister Nixon's public posture in defence of Red Deer's refusal of four-units-as-of-right, provincial approval may be more readily available for a retreat from federal supply mandates than at any earlier point — but federal approval would simultaneously become harder. The two-key structure means a council pursuing a retreat must plan for a multi-month, multi-stakeholder process with no guarantee of either approval.

6.6 Defensible alternatives — ranked by legal risk and HAF compatibility

The legally cleanest path for managing operational concentration concerns is a registration regime for secondary suites paired with parcel-level parking and design standards, and targeted business-licence regulation for STR-operated suites. The full ranking, from lowest to highest risk:

Mechanism Legal defensibility HAF risk Primary residual risk
Form-based design standards (lot coverage, FAR, height, setbacks, building-envelope rules) High None None significant
Registration / business-licence regime High None — operational only Enforcement adequacy (see Toronto AG audit, §7.3)
Owner-occupancy on STR licences only High None — STR-specific None significant if calibrated
Vacancy-trigger moratorium on new non-primary STR licences (Calgary 2.5% mechanism) High None — bylaw-conditioned, objectively triggered, prospective only, reversible None significant
Parcel-level parking standards Medium-High Tension above HAF baseline Reversing Airdrie's HAF parking commitment
Lot-size and lot-width minimums (calibrated) Medium Tension above HAF baseline Adverse-effect exposure on smaller / older lots
Separation distance (nuisance-grounded) Medium Tier 2 risk if it constrains supply Alcoholism Foundation / OHRC if not nuisance-grounded
Parcel-level discretionary proximity considerations at the development-permit stage (Tier 4) Medium Low — Tier 4 in HAF taxonomy Must be paired with §4.5 displacement acknowledgment and §6.3 AHRA analysis

The recommended package below operates entirely in the High-defensibility / Low-HAF-risk rows of this table.

6.7 The recommended package

The recommended package, drawn from peer best practice and conditional on a 12-month Airdrie-specific measurement program (§8.2), comprises four elements.

(a) Suite registration and inventory regime. A registration requirement for all accessory suites in Airdrie (secondary, garage, garden), drawn from peer practice in Calgary (which has registered 20,000+ suites by mid-2025), Edmonton, and most strategic-context jurisdictions. Registration is the cleanest mechanism for building the data infrastructure that any future calibrated density-management measure would require — including data on parcel distribution, occupancy patterns, parking impacts, and tenure composition. Registration is HAF-compatible (it does not reduce supply), legally robust (it has the strongest case-law support among density-management instruments), and supports the §8.2 measurement program by construction.

(b) Parcel-level form-based standards. Lot coverage, floor-area ratio, building height, side-yard setback, and built-form envelope rules calibrated to the operational concerns Airdrie's council has flagged (parking impact near schools, neighbour notification on density change). These regulate built form, not use, and are doctrinally insulated from Bell challenge. They cap intensity per parcel without capping suites per block. The current B-30/2024 parking standard (one stall per suite + two for principal dwelling) is at the permissive end of the Alberta peer band; tightening would put Airdrie above peer norms and could engage HAF parking commitments — calibration should be defensive of the existing B-30/2024 baseline.

(c) STR bylaw modelled on Rocky View C-8640-2025. A first-time STR bylaw establishing a primary-residence-required regime with three-document verification (land title + government ID + utility records), a tiered 1-year → 3-year → 5-year permit ladder rewarding compliance, a mandatory noise mitigation and site-management plan as a condition of permit issuance, an HOA/condo overlay, an on-parcel-only guest parking rule, and a single-tier $150–$250 annual licence fee with a $100–$150 fire inspection. The bylaw should include an equity-adjusted fee design (sliding fee scale tied to gross STR revenue; micro-operator exemption for spare-room hosts under a low-volume threshold; simplified-registration short form for low-volume operators) per Stream E §11.1.

(d) Vacancy-trigger moratorium on new non-primary STR licences. Calgary-style mechanism: no new non-primary STR licences issued when Airdrie's purpose-built rental vacancy rate, as published in the annual CMHC Rental Market Report, falls below a defined threshold (Calgary uses 2.5%; Airdrie should calibrate to local CMHC vacancy data). The mechanism is prospective only, objectively triggered, rationally connected to a stated planning purpose, and reversible — the four properties Stream F identifies as making vacancy-trigger pauses doctrinally robust.

STR-in-suites design. Rather than a precautionary outright prohibition, the recommended first-day rule (per Stream E §11.2) permits STR operation in a secondary suite when the suite is the host's principal residence. A contingency clause authorises staff to bring forward an amendment prohibiting STR-in-suites outright if either (a) suite-to-STR conversion in Airdrie exceeds a defined threshold (e.g., 5% of new suites built under B-30/2024 + B-06/2025 in any 12-month period) or (b) Airdrie's purpose-built rental vacancy drops below the moratorium threshold. This contingency design is the standard housing-protection move when the evidence base is thin: it commits council to act when the conversion threat materialises, without imposing an income-control measure on owner-occupant suite hosts before any conversion has been documented.

The package is HAF-safe across all four tiers, MGA-compliant (operates within s. 640 LUB authority and Part 9 business-licence authority), and politically defensible against neighbourhood-character pushback because every element has an outcome metric and a sunset/review trigger. It does not require Alberta provincial legislation Airdrie does not have.

7. Evidence and Outcomes

7.1 Edmonton — first-year data under Zoning Bylaw 20001

Edmonton's Zoning Bylaw 20001 came into force on January 1, 2024 — the first comprehensive rewrite in over 60 years. The RS Small Scale Residential Zone allows up to eight units per residential lot by default, with variations based on lot size, location, and transit proximity (City of Edmonton, n.d.-a, n.d.-b; Storeys, 2025; Taproot Edmonton, 2025).

First-year outcome data (synthesised from City of Edmonton ZB 20001 permit data via Storeys and corroborated by Taproot Edmonton):

  • 555 total development permits in 2024 in RS-eligible categories.
  • Single-detached: 159 (28.6%).
  • Single-detached + secondary suite: 100 (18.0%).
  • Row houses: 165 (29.7%).
  • Semi-detached: 117 (21.1%).
  • Multiplexes: only 14 permits (2.5%).
  • Row-house unit growth: 1,216 new units in 2024 vs. 146 units annually average 2019–2023 — an eightfold increase.
  • Backyard housing: 177 permits → 258 units.
  • Total missing-middle housing more than doubled: ~1,000 units in 2023 to ~2,300 units in 2024.

The single most useful data point for an undecided council: of 242 development permits issued for sites meeting eight-unit capacity, only 122 developers (50.4%) opted for the maximum density. City staff noted that "a variety of different housing forms are being approved and the maximum allowable unit density is not always sought out" (Storeys, 2025). Even when density is fully unlocked to the highest level among Canadian comparators, slightly less than half of eligible permits go to the ceiling. This is empirical refutation of the "if you allow it, everyone will build to the max everywhere" objection.

Confidence flag. The Edmonton 50.4% finding is sourced via two independent secondary outlets (Storeys, Taproot Edmonton) reproducing the same underlying City of Edmonton ZB 20001 first-year permit dataset. The Citation Verifier treated this as primary-corroborated; the underlying dataset is the authoritative source. It is a single year of single-city data — recommend pairing with the Storeys + Taproot two-source corroboration when cited and avoiding overweighting against other interventions (Stress Test residual risk R4).

7.2 Cochrane — supply response post-cap-removal

Cochrane Council removed its 10% neighbourhood cap on February 10, 2020, replacing it with a "street-scale" cumulative-impact test that considers parking congestion, utility-services capacity, and the cumulative presence of home-based businesses on the street (Boon, 2020). The amendment passed "without modifications" after the January 27, 2020 public hearing — no councillor amendments tightened or loosened the staff proposal. Cochrane's Development Summary for 2024 reports secondary-suite applications grew 232% in 2024 (95 applications; 39.3% of overall development application growth) (Town of Cochrane, 2025).

The growth correlates with Cochrane's broader population expansion (toward 40,000) and the federal/provincial housing-supply policy environment. The growth cannot be attributed solely to the cap removal. Even so, it does not support the hypothesis that removing a cap produces neighbourhood-character collapse: no published spike in complaints, no rollback motions, no documented enforcement-displacement events appear in the post-2020 record. This is the cleanest Alberta before/after evidence available for the cap-removal scenario.

7.3 Toronto — Auditor General audit findings

The Toronto Auditor General's 2024 audit (published 2024-06-20) is the most consequential evidence in the scan for the enforcement-adequacy question (Toronto Auditor General, 2024). Verbatim figures verified against the audit report:

  • 1,438 operators may have exceeded the 180-night rule.
  • 545 operators may have exceeded the 3-bedroom rule.
  • 170 owners may have more than one short-term rental property (the multi-property finding).
  • 1,100 registered homeowners had a different mailing address for property tax than for the STR address — i.e., the principal-residence claim was potentially fraudulent on the face of City records. This is a separate finding from the multi-property count; the property-tax-mailing-address evidence speaks to principal-residence verification weakness, not to portfolio operation.
  • At least 10% of transactions undetected by licensed companies (i.e., not flowing through Airbnb's MAT collection).
  • At least 250 potentially unregistered or illegal STRs advertising on Craigslist, Kijiji, Facebook, or unlicensed STR platforms.

The audit characterised enforcement as "challenging" because of "limited resources, outdated techniques in data analysis, and highly manual, labour-intensive internal processes." Toronto added 23 new FTEs in April 2024 in response. The lesson for Airdrie is that a strong-on-paper STR rule does not enforce itself: the volume of suspected non-compliance scaled with enforcement capacity, not with bylaw stringency. Toronto's three-document-plus-interview proof-of-residence requirement (Phase 2, in force 2024-09-30) is a defensible template that does not depend on a provincial registry to function.

7.4 BC and Vancouver — supply-protection evidence

The McGill UPGo / Statistics Canada estimates indicate approximately 16,000 BC homes shifted from residential to dedicated commercial STR in 2022, contributing to a 19% decline in housing availability and a 28% rise in rents in affected markets (UPGo McGill, n.d.; Globalnews.ca, n.d.). Bill 35 was the policy response. By mid-2025, BC had ~20,000 registered listings against the 2023 baseline of ~28,000 — a compression of roughly 8,000 listings within 12 months of enforcement (STAY Magazine, 2025).

Vancouver's pre-Bill 35 enforcement converted or delisted ~400 STR units; prosecution was initiated against commercial operators representing 89 listings (potential fines up to $890,000) (City of Vancouver, n.d.).

Calibration caveat for Airdrie. These numbers come from tight-vacancy, high-tourism BC markets. Airdrie is a commuter suburb of Calgary with a purpose-built rental vacancy historically running 3–5% and no tourism-anchored STR economy. The proportional supply gain from a strong STR regime in Airdrie is going to be modest in absolute terms — perhaps tens of units, not hundreds. The case for the §6.7 baseline package rests on its HAF-safety and regulatory-readiness properties, not on a Vancouver-scale supply recovery (Stream E §11.4). The BC compression should be attributed to the package of factors driving it (Bill 35 + interest-rate environment + Airbnb commercial-host crackdown + tourism rebalancing), not to the principal-residence rule alone.

7.5 Edmonton — STR complaint volume as proportional indicator

Edmonton's council file UPE02476 documented that STR-related nuisance complaints represent 0.3% of total bylaw complaints, while licensed STRs grew 70% since May 2024 (CBC News, 2025; City of Edmonton, n.d.-c). Administration explored principal-residence-only restriction, host-on-premises requirement, and a three-strike rule, but recommended against additional regulatory measures, citing procedural-fairness concerns, jurisdictional issues, privacy concerns, and enforcement burden. The recommendation was for a public-education campaign rather than enhanced regulation. This is the cleanest "lighter-touch" Alberta peer data point; the 0.3% figure scales to absolute volume and that absolute volume is small relative to Edmonton's bylaw-services workload, not necessarily small in proportion to Airdrie's.

8. Conclusion

8.1 Evidence-based conclusion — caps vs alternatives

The evidence accumulated across the six streams, three red teams, citation verification, and stress test supports a clear conclusion: a HAF-safe package combining suite registration, parcel-level form-based standards, a primary-residence STR bylaw modelled on Rocky View C-8640-2025, and a vacancy-trigger moratorium on non-primary STR licences is the defensible policy choice for the City of Airdrie at this point in the legislative calendar. The package addresses every operational concern raised in the engagement record around B-30/2024 — parking saturation, neighbourhood character, infrastructure capacity — through instruments that operate on the use and form of dwelling units rather than on their existence. It does not put HAF tranches three and four at risk. It does not require Alberta provincial legislation Airdrie does not have. It substitutes evidence-based reversibility (annual outcome reporting against quantified targets; vacancy-trigger pauses; contingency clauses tied to documented conversion data) for the political reversibility a concentration cap purports to provide.

A net-new neighbourhood numerical concentration cap (Tier 2 in the §6.4 taxonomy) is the highest-risk available mechanism. It combines five compounding risk vectors: operational displacement at the point of enforcement (the first-order equity harm, §4.5); plausible Charter s. 15 adverse-effect exposure based on the Alcoholism Foundation line of authority; AHRA s. 5 exposure on family-status, source-of-income, and disability grounds running independent of the Charter pathway; reasonableness-review exposure where the calibration record is thin; and HAF instalment-pause risk that turns on whether the cap materially constrains the supply targets to which Airdrie has already committed. The Calgary letter of February 10, 2026 is the controlling precedent for Tier 2 risk; the functional-equivalence standard in that letter applies to all housing forms under the HAF Contribution Agreement, not specifically to secondary suites.

If Council nevertheless wishes to test the limits, the most defensible cap-adjacent surface is a Tier 4 parcel-level discretionary proximity tool modelled on Red Deer's Location Criteria — proximity considerations applied at the development-permit stage on Discretionary applications, with a documented evidentiary record on what the proximity considerations are intended to address (parking saturation, fire access, servicing capacity). This carries materially less HAF exposure than a Tier 2 numerical cap and must be paired with the §4.5 enforcement-displacement acknowledgment and the §6.3 AHRA analysis.

8.2 The recommendation is conditional on a 12-month Airdrie-specific measurement program

The recommended package is not a finished bylaw text. It is a regulatory architecture conditional on Airdrie's specific local conditions. The dossier was produced without primary Airdrie data on STR listing count, parking-saturation observations, suite-related complaint volume, fiscal model, or rental-stock conversion baseline. The Phase 2 calibration of the package must follow the measurement, not precede it.

The 12-month measurement program should encompass:

  1. STR listing count and pattern — a compliance-vendor contract producing monthly counts of Airdrie STR listings, geocoded to parcels, classified by tenure (primary vs non-primary), with a comparative against the Rocky View County baseline at the boundary.
  2. Suite-to-STR conversion rate — for each suite issued a development permit under B-30/2024 and B-06/2025, a 12-month follow-up determining whether the suite is operated as long-term rental, as STR, or vacant. This is the data point that triggers the §6.7 (d) contingency clause if conversion exceeds the defined threshold.
  3. Complaint volume and geographic distribution — bylaw-services data on suite-related and STR-related complaints, parsed by neighbourhood, with a comparative against the pre-B-30/2024 baseline.
  4. Parking saturation observations — focused on the neighbourhoods near schools that Mayor Spearman has flagged operationally, plus the cul-de-sac and semi-detached configurations that remain Discretionary under B-30/2024.
  5. Renter consultation — engagement with Community Links, Airdrie Housing Limited, and Calgary-CMA newcomer service providers, satisfying the methodological limitation flagged at §2.5 above.
  6. No-action baseline — the consequences of inaction (estimated supply trajectory under B-30/2024 + B-06/2025 alone, with no further intervention), measured against each candidate intervention.

The Phase 2 stress test should re-evaluate the §6.7 recommended package against the measurement data before any final bylaw drafting reaches council. This is the single most important hardening move the deliverables can carry, and it is the framing that addresses both the senior-advisor and the industry-counsel critiques surfaced in Red Team 1 and Red Team 2.

8.3 Residual risks and acknowledged gaps

The principal residual risks the integration team and council should hold:

  • The recommended package is regulatory infrastructure, not a Vancouver-scale supply recovery. Stating the scale honestly ("tens of units, not hundreds") is the credibility-protective framing in front of industry rebuttal.
  • Single-cause attribution language is a precision trap. BC's compression (28,000 → 20,000 listings) should be attributed to the package of factors (Bill 35 + interest-rate environment + Airbnb commercial-host crackdown + tourism rebalancing), not to the principal-residence rule alone.
  • Rocky View C-8640-2025 has been in force less than one year. The recommendation cites Rocky View as a template; the implementation specification should carry the under-one-year track-record caveat.
  • Indigenous housing rights and newcomer household vulnerability are an acknowledged gap. Engagement with regional Indigenous housing organisations and newcomer-services providers is a precondition for any cap-family mechanism (Stream F §2.5); for the HAF-safe baseline package the engagement bar is lower because the package expands rather than constrains supply, but the gap should be explicit on the record. Specifically, prior to adopting any cap-family mechanism, Airdrie's planning department should engage directly with (a) the Airdrie Local Immigration Partnership or equivalent newcomer-services coordinator, and (b) regional Indigenous housing organisations including the Aboriginal Friendship Centre of Calgary, the Métis Nation of Alberta Region 3 housing portfolio, and any Tsuut'ina / Stoney Nakoda housing liaison contacts available through Treaty 7 / Indigenous Relations channels.
  • Provincial Bill 18 procedural risk applies to any HAF Action Plan amendment. A two-key approval pathway (CMHC + Alberta Cabinet) attaches to any retreat from the supply commitments. The recommended package avoids this pathway by construction.
