Council Briefing — Secondary Suite Density and Short-Term Rentals

Prepared for: Mayor Heather Spearman and Members of Council, City of Airdrie
From: Karto Research, on behalf of City of Airdrie staff briefing the council
Date: May 11, 2026
Briefing window: Community Infrastructure and Strategic Growth Standing Committee, Tuesday May 12, 1 p.m.; four-homes-per-lot public consultation closes May 25
Reading time: 15 minutes
Status: Decision-ready

How to read this briefing

This is written to be read once, the morning of a meeting. It will not ask you to remember statute numbers, bylaw section references, or comparative tables. Those are in the technical report behind this document, and staff can answer to them.

What follows is the question council has been asked, what comparable councils have done, what the realistic risks are, what the Housing Accelerator Fund agreement means for this file, and what the evidence supports. The recommendation is at the end.

1. The question before council

You have been asked — and, on the four-homes-per-lot consultation closing May 25, will shortly be asked formally — to decide two related things.

First: whether Airdrie should regulate how many secondary suites can exist in a neighbourhood. This is sometimes called a "concentration cap." The version of this question that has been raised in public conversation is whether one neighbourhood can end up with too high a share of suites relative to single-family homes.

Second: whether short-term rentals — rooms or whole homes rented through platforms like Airbnb and VRBO — need a licensing framework in Airdrie. The city does not currently have one.

These two questions are linked because both touch the same underlying concern: how much change a neighbourhood absorbs, and over what timeline. They are also linked because a suite that is rented short-term is not contributing to long-term housing supply — which is the commitment Airdrie has made under its federal funding agreement.

This briefing addresses both.

2. What comparable councils have done

The research scan covered twenty municipalities across Canada. The pattern is unambiguous.

No large Canadian city uses a neighbourhood concentration cap on secondary suites. Not Calgary. Not Edmonton. Not Vancouver. Not Toronto. Each of these cities has chosen, instead, to manage suites through registration, building standards, parking rules, and short-term-rental licensing.

One mid-sized Alberta municipality does. Red Deer adopted a 15 percent neighbourhood cap on combined house and backyard suites in 2024, with a tighter 10 percent cap in five named heritage neighbourhoods. The cap was calibrated against Red Deer's own data — of 61 neighbourhoods, 9 exceeded 10 percent and 2 (South Hill and West Park east) exceeded 15 percent. The cap was not chosen abstractly. It was anchored to a documented local distribution.

One Alberta peer previously had a cap and removed it. Cochrane had a 10 percent neighbourhood limit on secondary suites and removed it in February 2020. In the four years since, secondary suite applications in Cochrane grew 232 percent. There is no public record of the council reversing that decision or the community organizing against it.

Edmonton's data answers the "what if we just allow more?" question directly. Edmonton allows up to eight units on most residential lots. In 2024, of the development permits issued on lots eligible for the maximum, only about half — roughly 50 percent — actually built to maximum density. The other half built less. The fear that opening density rules means every yard becomes the maximum is empirically unsupported in the only Canadian city where this has been tested for a full year.

On short-term rentals, the pattern is the reverse — most jurisdictions of any size now license them. Calgary launched a tiered licensing framework in April 2025. Edmonton has a licensing regime but recently chose not to tighten it further. Vancouver and Toronto have stricter rules backed by provincial frameworks Alberta does not have. The starting-tier toolkit — a licence, a principal-residence requirement, and a maximum nightly count — is common, well-tested, and not legally controversial.

3. What the evidence supports — and what it doesn't

The evidence supports licensing short-term rentals. There is no documented case of a Canadian municipality regretting this decision. Calgary's framework was unanimous at council. Edmonton's data shows short-term-rental complaints sit at 0.3 percent of total bylaw complaints — small in proportion, but real, and a licensing regime is what allows the city to know what it is actually dealing with.

The evidence does not support a neighbourhood concentration cap on suites at this time. This is not because caps are illegal — they are not. Red Deer's cap survives because it is grounded in observed data. The evidence does not support a cap in Airdrie because Airdrie does not yet have the data Red Deer had. Without that data, a cap would be vulnerable to challenge — the precise vulnerability Red Deer avoided.

The evidence supports registration of suites. Calgary has more than 20,000 registered suites and the registry has become a reliable basis for both safety inspections and policy decisions. Registration does not restrict supply. It produces data.

The evidence is mixed on standalone parking and setback adjustments. These can address operational concerns where they exist, but they are not a substitute for either licensing or measurement. They are tools, not a strategy.

4. Key risks of different approaches

Approach A — Numerical concentration cap (Red Deer model)

What it does: Limits the number of secondary suites in any one neighbourhood to a fixed share of houses.

Legal exposure: Defensible if backed by Airdrie-specific data. Vulnerable to challenge if adopted on a comparable-city argument or community sentiment alone. The question a court asks is "why this number, in this place?" and the record needs to answer it.

Federal funding exposure: This is the file council needs to understand most carefully. Red Deer's federal housing agreement was cancelled in January 2026. The cap was not why. Red Deer's federal agreement was signed with the 15 percent cap already in force, and the federal government accepted it. What cost Red Deer the funding was the separate council vote in July 2025 refusing to allow four units of housing per lot citywide. Airdrie's situation is different: Airdrie has already committed to four units per lot under its federal agreement. So a new cap adopted by Airdrie would be evaluated against a different baseline. The risk depends on whether the cap meaningfully reduces the housing units Airdrie has already promised. If it does, the federal exposure is real.

Equity exposure: This is the risk that is least visible at the moment a cap is adopted, and most visible at the moment it is enforced. When a cap is in force and a neighbourhood is at or over the limit, the practical enforcement action is to refuse new suites — or, in some versions, to require existing unpermitted suites to come into compliance. The research record on this is consistent: enforcement displaces existing tenants, and those tenants are disproportionately elderly and lower-income. The harm lands on the renter before it lands anywhere else.

Approach B — No action

What it does: Continues current policy. Suites are permitted across most residential districts (under Bylaws B-30/2024 and B-06/2025). No short-term-rental licensing.

Legal and federal funding exposure: None. This is the safest path on both fronts.

Cost: Neighbourhood-character concerns remain unaddressed. The 33 percent rise in suite applications between January–May 2025 indicates real uptake — and real change in some neighbourhoods. Council leaves itself without a current evidence base if pushback materializes later. The short-term-rental question continues to operate without a regulatory framework.

Approach C — Licensing and measurement now; concentration mechanism considered later only if needed

What it does: Three pieces, sequenced:

  1. Short-term rental licensing framework now. A primary-residence requirement, a maximum nightly count, and an annual licence. Calgary's tiered fee model — lower fee for primary residence, higher fee for non-primary — is directly importable.
  2. Suite registration. A mandatory registration step at occupancy. Operational, not supply-restricting. Produces the data Airdrie does not currently have.
  3. A 12-month measurement baseline. Before any concentration mechanism is considered, the city collects what Red Deer had: which neighbourhoods are at what concentration, what the complaint pattern is, where parking saturation is real and where it is anecdotal.

Legal exposure: Low. Each of these tools is well-tested across Canadian municipalities.

Federal funding exposure: None — none of these mechanisms reduces the housing supply Airdrie has committed to.

Equity exposure: Low. None of these tools triggers displacement.

Cost: Council does not have a concentration-control mechanism in force during the measurement year. Council retains the option to adopt one — with a stronger record — at the end of that year.

5. What your federal funding agreement means for this decision

Airdrie signed a $24.8 million federal Housing Accelerator Fund agreement in 2024. Roughly half of those funds have already been received. The agreement commits the city to seven local initiatives, including permitting four units per lot citywide and reducing parking minimums for suites — most of which are already in force or under active consideration.

The agreement is not a grant. It is a contractual arrangement with reporting milestones and clawback risk if the city does not deliver on the commitments. The federal government cancelled Red Deer's agreement in January 2026 — recovering $12 million in funding — because Red Deer's council voted against the four-units-per-lot commitment. That is the most direct case study available, and it is recent.

For council's purposes, the practical reading is this: any mechanism that materially constrains the housing supply Airdrie has committed to is a contractual risk that needs to be evaluated against the agreement. Cosmetic mechanisms — licensing, registration, parking adjustments, setback rules — do not constrain supply and are not at risk. A concentration cap that meaningfully reduces the number of suites the city would otherwise approve does constrain supply, and it should not be adopted without first sequencing it through the federal funder.

This is not an argument against ever adopting a cap. It is an argument for being deliberate about the sequence: measure, document, consult the funder, then adopt — not the reverse.

There is a second layer. The Government of Alberta's Provincial Priorities Act took effect April 1, 2025. Any amendment to Airdrie's federal agreement — including any change council might want to make to the action plan to add density limits — would now require provincial cabinet review because the agreement value exceeds the threshold. The political optics of asking the Smith government for permission to retreat from a federal housing commitment are something council should consider before initiating that path.

6. Legal exposure — the short version

If council adopts a concentration cap without a documented Airdrie-specific evidence base, the cap is vulnerable to challenge on the question "why this number, in this place?" Without an answer, the cap can be struck down as unreasonable. With an answer — the kind of empirical calibration Red Deer did — a cap is defensible.

The recommended sequence is therefore not "should we cap or not?" but "what is the evidence base required, and have we built it?" This is the planner's version of the same question lawyers will ask if the bylaw is ever challenged.

There is a second exposure council should know about. A cap that names neighbourhoods, references "neighbourhood character," or correlates in practice with the share of renters, lower-income residents, or any protected group runs an equity exposure that the courts have been increasingly willing to examine. The drafting matters. Council does not need to resolve this today, but should resolve it before any cap is brought forward for a vote.

7. Recommendation

The evidence supports the following sequence.

  1. Adopt a short-term-rental licensing framework now. It is HAF-safe, low-risk, has no supply impact, and addresses a real regulatory gap. Calgary's framework is the most directly importable template.

  2. Adopt a secondary suite registration requirement. Operational, not supply-restricting, and produces the data the city does not currently have.

  3. Establish a 12-month measurement baseline. Track suite concentrations by neighbourhood, complaint patterns, parking impacts, and short-term-rental uptake. Report back to council at the end of the period.

  4. Defer any decision on a concentration cap until after that measurement period. If, at the end of 12 months, the data shows concentrations in specific neighbourhoods that warrant intervention, council will have what Red Deer had — a defensible record. If the data does not show that, council will have answered the question without paying a federal-funding or legal-challenge cost to do it.

This sequence does not foreclose any future option. It does ensure that whatever council eventually decides is grounded in Airdrie's own evidence.

8. Three questions to ask at the May 12 committee meeting

If council wants to test the readiness of the staff package before this comes forward for a vote, three questions will surface what matters.

1. Who on staff is the file lead for this item, and have they reviewed the federal Housing Accelerator Fund agreement implications of any density-control mechanism council might consider?

2. Do we have Airdrie-specific data on complaint volumes, parking saturation by neighbourhood, and current suite concentrations? If not, when will we?

3. What is the full range of regulatory options being prepared for council's consideration, and what is the legal, financial, and equity risk profile of each?

If staff can answer the first question without hesitation, the file has a clear owner. If staff can answer the second affirmatively, council can move directly to options. If staff cannot answer the second affirmatively, the measurement-first sequence above is the most defensible path forward.

9. What this briefing is not

This briefing is not a recommendation against secondary suites. The evidence is clear that suites contribute to housing supply, generate income for homeowners, and — when registered — are safe and well-managed. The 33 percent rise in Airdrie applications in early 2025 reflects real demand from real homeowners.

This briefing is also not a recommendation against ever adopting a concentration mechanism. Red Deer's mechanism is defensible. The question is what evidence base supports that kind of decision in Airdrie, and whether the city has built it yet.

The briefing is a recommendation to sequence the decision — to license what is clearly licensable now, measure what needs to be measured, and revisit the harder question with a stronger record in twelve months.
