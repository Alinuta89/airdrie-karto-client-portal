How to use this document

This briefing is written for the planner working the suite-density / STR file day-to-day. It is the most technically detailed of the three KT-018 audience briefings. It is not a summary of the technical report — it is a purpose-built decision-support document that:

  1. Names every mechanism on the table and rates each against the four binding constraints (MGA s. 642, HAF, Charter/AHRA, equity).
  2. Gives the comparative basis a planner can defend in a CISG Standing Committee briefing, with the citations on the page rather than buried in appendices.
  3. Names the enforcement and displacement risks plainly so the recommendation that goes upward does not produce a surprise at the file's first contact with council.
  4. Maps the four ranked policy options and gives the framing language to use with the CISG committee (Cllr. Chapman chair), the SDAB chair (Cllr. Kolson), the swing-vote new councillors (Glass, Shima, Obasan, Stewart), and the mayor.

1. What is being asked

Airdrie is in the live policy window between Phase 1 of the four-homes-per-lot consultation (closes 2026-05-25) and the drafting of the bylaw text that flows from it. Two adjacent regulatory questions are already on the table and a third is anticipated:

File Status Mechanism on the table

Secondary suite density / concentration concern Anticipated — surfaces in any public engagement on intensification; raised in Spearman's parking-near-schools comments and Petrow's 2024 dissent Numerical concentration cap (Red Deer model), parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria, separation distance, or parking-via-built-form
STR regulation Regulatory greenfield — no Airdrie STR bylaw exists Business-licence bylaw with principal-residence rule (Calgary / Rocky View model); possibly LUB definition

The KT-018 research question, framed by the client request, is whether geographic concentration caps on secondary suites are commonly used and defensible in Canadian municipal practice, and what alternatives exist. The honest answer the dossier surfaces:

Geographic concentration caps on suites are not common in Canadian practice (Red Deer is the only published, in-force example in mid-sized Alberta; Cochrane removed its previous 10% cap in 2020; no major Canadian city outside Alberta uses a numerical neighbourhood cap on suites). Caps are legally vulnerable on reasonableness, Charter s. 15 adverse-effect, and AHRA s. 5 grounds, and they are operationally costly because they invert the enforcement burden onto the renter rather than the property owner. Defensible alternatives — parking standards, lot dimensions, separation distances grounded in measurable nuisance criteria, registration regimes, design standards, and STR business-licence controls — exist and are widely used.

This is the bottom line the planner is briefing upward. The rest of this document is the supporting scaffolding.

2.2 What this table is showing the planner

Three things, all defensible from this evidence base:

  1. The cleanest HAF-safe density-management family is design standards + parking standards + registration + STR principal-residence licence. Each tool in that family sits in Tier 1 (HAF-compliant calibration) or Tier 4 (not a supply gate). Combined they do not retreat from B-30/2024 or the four-units-as-of-right HAF commitment; they manage operational impact at the margin.

  2. The Red Deer numerical cap is doable but expensive. It requires empirical calibration on the record (Stream F §4.2 explains why an arbitrary threshold fails Vavilov reasonableness review). It triggers Charter s. 15 + AHRA s. 5 adverse-effect exposure. It requires a neighbourhood-level tracking system. Red Deer's cap survived CMHC's HAF-2 due diligence (it pre-dated the agreement); a post-HAF cap in Airdrie sits in Tier 2 of the HAF taxonomy and carries variable cancellation exposure depending on whether it measurably constrains the agreed supply target.

  3. Parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria are the lowest HAF risk among cap-family tools because they operate at the DP stage on Discretionary applications, not as a flat neighbourhood ceiling. Airdrie's surviving Discretionary triggers (cul-de-sac, semi-detached) are the natural insertion point. The legal risk shifts to SDAB-appeal exposure rather than judicial-review exposure.

3. What peer municipalities did and why

The 20-municipality scan in the technical report yields four directly load-bearing comparators. The Stream B comparators (Toronto, Vancouver) are context-only because they operate under BC Bill 44 / Bill 35 and Ontario Bill 23 statutory floors that Alberta does not have.

3.1 Red Deer — the only published in-force Alberta numerical cap

Mechanism (verified via Stream A §3.6, Stream C §1, Stream F §4.2): Red Deer's Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 operates a layered two-tier control:

  • §4.50.5 — Neighbourhood numerical cap: 15% cap on the combined count of House Suites and Backyard Suites per neighbourhood, calibrated to the observed distribution (9 of 61 neighbourhoods exceeded 10% suite-to-single-detached share; 2 of 61 exceeded 15% — South Hill and West Park east).
  • Parcel-level Location Criteria: Discretionary proximity considerations at 100 m radius, block, street, and row of properties, applied at the DP stage on Discretionary applications.

HAF outcome: CMHC signed HAF-2 with both instruments already in force. Red Deer's HAF-2 cancellation (effective 2026-01-16) was triggered by Red Deer Council's July 2025 unanimous vote not to adopt four-units-as-of-right — not by the cap or by the Location Criteria. The four-tier HAF taxonomy in §2.1 above operationalises this distinction.

Planner takeaway: Red Deer is a worked example of Alberta-permissive concentration controls, not a cautionary tale. The cautionary tale is on the expansion side: refusing four-units-as-of-right was the trigger.

Caveat for any council briefing material: A planner who frames Red Deer as a "cap precedent Airdrie should follow" will be attacked on three flanks: (a) the cap pre-dated HAF-2 — Airdrie's would be Tier 2 not Tier 3; (b) the cap was empirically calibrated to observed distribution — Airdrie has no parallel calibration on the record; (c) Red Deer ZB 3357/2024 is the only Alberta example, so the precedent base is thin. Industry-witness rebuttal (Red Team 2) will press exactly these points.

3.2 Cochrane — the only Canadian cap-removal precedent

Mechanism (verified via Stream C §5): Cochrane previously operated a 10% neighbourhood concentration cap on secondary suites. The Town removed the cap in February 2020 amendments to the predecessor LUB, replacing it with a street-scale cumulative-impact test. The current LUB 01/2022 carries forward the no-numeric-cap regime.

Outcome data: Secondary suite applications increased 232% in 2024 (95 applications versus the pre-removal baseline). No documented backlash sufficient to reverse the change.

Planner takeaway: Cochrane is the indispensable case study for any recommendation that does not adopt a cap. If council asks "what happens if we don't cap concentration?" — Cochrane is the data point. A 232% increase in applications post-cap-removal with no documented organised opposition is the cleanest available Alberta evidence on the supply-side effect of releasing the cap.

3.3 Calgary — the April 2026 repeal-with-amendment manoeuvre

Mechanism (verified via Stream A §3.1, Stream B "Calgary" section): Calgary Council approved repeal of blanket rezoning on April 8, 2026 (effective August 4, 2026) — while simultaneously directing Administration to bring forward amendments making secondary suites and backyard suites Permitted Use in all low-density residential districts. The public hearing on the operational scaffolding is scheduled for 2026-07-21.

HAF outcome: CMHC reaffirmed Calgary will receive the third HAF installment ($64.7M) conditional on the City adopting a zoning replacement allowing a minimum of four units on a lot for "a significant majority of lots across the city," in force or significantly developed by 2026-10-27. The fourth installment (~$60M) remains conditional on the replacement plan.

Planner takeaway for Airdrie: Calgary has executed a HAF-compatible retreat from blanket parcel-level rezoning by adding density through suites in all low-density districts. The HAF target is unit count, not unit typology. Airdrie's analogue — should political pressure on parcel-level rezoning materialise during four-homes-per-lot consultation — is to preserve the four-units-as-of-right commitment through suite permissions while introducing concentration management via Tier 4 design standards rather than cap-family tools.

3.4 Rocky View County — adjacent STR template (C-8640-2025)

Mechanism (verified via Stream E §1.3, §2.1): Rocky View County's Bylaw C-8640-2025 (in force July 8, 2025) treats vacation rental as a Discretionary use countywide, conditional on three-document principal-residence verification (land title + government ID + utility records). Uses a tiered temporary development permit (1-year → 3-year → 5-year) with complaint-history-based renewal escalation. Occupancy / parking / noise plan required.

Planner takeaway: Rocky View is the directly importable STR template for Airdrie because (a) the regulatory environment is identical (MGA Part 9 + Part 17), (b) the operator universe overlaps with Airdrie's, and (c) the bylaw has been through public engagement (448 surveys + 2,226 comments in fall 2024 — the comments file is publicly available and is a useful reference for the language of community concerns that surface in this engagement). The Rocky View principal-residence verification standard is more rigorous than most municipal regimes and gives staff clearer admin tooling than a self-declaration model.

3.5 Vancouver / Surrey / BC municipalities — do not transplant

Caveat (from Stream B "Vancouver" caveat and Stream D): Vancouver's STR architecture is largely set by BC Bill 35 (Short-Term Rental Accommodations Act, 2023), not by freely chosen municipal policy. Vancouver's suite density rules sit inside the BC Bill 44 SSMUH 3–6-unit floor. Alberta has no equivalent provincial mandates. Direct copying of Vancouver design choices ignores that the provincial scaffolding Vancouver operates inside does not exist in Alberta.

Planner takeaway: Cite Vancouver / Surrey / Victoria for outcome data (e.g., the 16,000 BC homes displaced; 8,000-listing compression after Bill 35) only with the proportionality caveat: those outcomes are tight-vacancy, high-tourism markets. Airdrie is a commuter suburb without a tourism anchor; the proportional supply-protection gain is "tens of units, not hundreds" (Stream E §11.4). Overstating the threat is a credibility risk in front of industry-witness rebuttal.

3.6 The other 14 jurisdictions in the scan

Briefly: Edmonton, Toronto, Halifax, Ottawa, Mississauga, Kitchener, St. Albert, Lethbridge, Medicine Hat, Okotoks, Saskatoon, Victoria, Kelowna, and Canmore are all surveyed in the technical report. The most useful data point from outside the four named comparators above is Edmonton's 50.4% maximum-density-uptake finding: of 242 development permits issued for sites meeting eight-unit capacity in 2024, only 122 (50.4%) chose maximum density. This is the empirical answer to the "if we allow more, every yard becomes a maxed-out site" concern — see §7 below on briefing the new councillors.

4. Airdrie-specific implementation considerations

This section is the planner-specific operational layer that does not appear in the council or ELT briefings. It names the four binding constraints and what each means for the bylaw drafting that will follow the four-homes-per-lot consultation.

4.1 MGA s. 642 — the one-way Permitted Use ratchet

Constraint: Bylaws B-30/2024 (adopted 2024-12-17) and B-06/2025 moved most secondary, garage, and garden suite types from Discretionary to Permitted Use across most residential districts. Under MGA s. 642, the development authority must issue a development permit for a Permitted Use that meets bylaw requirements. Council and SDAB have no discretionary refusal power for Permitted Uses.

What this means operationally:

  • Future density-control levers cannot rely on approval discretion. They must operate via objective, measurable bylaw criteria that the development authority applies as gatekeeping rules.
  • The realistic levers remaining are: parking minimums (currently 1 stall/suite — and any tightening reverses a HAF commitment), lot-size and lot-width minimums, lot coverage / floor-area ratios, separation distance, registration / business-licence requirements at occupancy, and reverting specific configurations back to Discretionary (politically expensive after a 33% application uptick in 2025; HAF-non-compliance risk).
  • The two surviving Discretionary triggers — semi-detached and cul-de-sac — are the only remaining lanes for parcel-level discretionary review. Adding Location Criteria to these Discretionary triggers (Red Deer style) is a viable lever without re-classifying any Permitted use back to Discretionary.

Planner action item: Any cap-family or Location-Criteria recommendation that depends on Discretionary refusal must specify which trigger (semi-detached, cul-de-sac, or a new trigger requiring bylaw amendment under MGA s. 692 with public hearing). A planner who recommends "neighbourhood-cap concentration review" without naming the s. 642 mechanic will be asked to explain it at the first CISG briefing — pre-empt that question.

4.2 HAF Contribution Agreement — the $24.8M binding contractual constraint

Constraint: Airdrie's HAF Contribution Agreement is $24.8M over four tranches; CMHC's public Action Plan Summary lists total estimated eligibility of $24.839M, structured as four annual advances of $6.20975M. Local reporting in the dossier states that, as of the October 2024 council update, $12.4M had been received and 39% of milestones were complete. Because this briefing is dated 2026-05-11 and the next advance was expected March 2026 on the public record available to the dossier, current disbursement status should be confirmed with the City or CMHC before client-facing use. Operational owners at staff level are Jessica DeVreeze (Affordable Housing Team Leader) and Cheryl Selinger (Housing Policy Strategist). The seven local initiatives in the Action Plan include: streamlining zoning bylaws to allow four units as-of-right, reducing parking restrictions and lot size for secondary suites, and a secondary-suite incentive program.

Two-key amendment pathway: Any amendment to the Action Plan — including adding density-restricting mechanisms that constrain agreed supply targets — now requires both:

  1. Federal renegotiation with CMHC (Calgary's April 2026 conditional approval is the procedural template);
  2. Alberta Cabinet approval under the Provincial Priorities Act (Bill 18, in force 2025-04-01) for HAF Action Plan amendments at this dollar scale (the $5M+ Cabinet threshold).

What this means operationally:

  • Any recommendation that retreats from four-units-as-of-right or from the parking-reduction commitment requires the two-key pathway. The procedural friction is significant; the political optics of asking the Smith government for permission to retreat from a federal housing deal are sensitive.
  • Tier 4 mechanisms (registration, STR business licence, design standards, parcel-level Location Criteria on existing Discretionary triggers) do not require the two-key pathway because they do not amend the Action Plan.
  • A post-HAF numerical concentration cap (Tier 2 in the §2.1 taxonomy) may require the two-key pathway if it measurably constrains the agreed supply target. The determinative question is empirical: does the cap materially reduce the four-units-as-of-right unit yield Airdrie has committed to deliver? Calgary's letter from CMHC (Feb 10, 2026) is the controlling precedent on the partial-non-compliance enforcement posture.

Planner action item: Any cap-family recommendation must include a HAF-traceability table mapping the cap's quantifiable yield impact against the Action Plan's unit-count targets. This is what Mayor Spearman will ask for first — she previously stated she wanted "more clarity around the city's Housing Accelerator Fund" before allocating additional funds (DiscoverAirdrie, airdries-affordable-housing-principled-action-plan-where-are-we-now).

4.3 No named Planning Director — staff accountability gap

Constraint: The Landscape Scan flagged that the City of Airdrie public website does not surface a single named "Planning Director" or "GM Planning & Development" for the 2025–2026 organizational structure. Stephen Utz's elevation to Director of the City Manager's Office appears to be the closest analogue; Jeff Brunen led the B-30/2024 file at Senior Planner level.

What this means operationally:

  • A council-facing recommendation that names a director without verifying the current org structure exposes the planner to an avoidable correction at the briefing table.
  • Internal briefings up to CAO (Horacio Galanti) should clarify file ownership before any council-facing material is finalised. If a Planning Director position has been filled but not surfaced on airdrie.ca, that should be confirmed through internal channels before publication.

Planner action item: Confirm the current GM / Director of Planning & Development by name and title through internal channels (HR org chart, City Manager's Office) before any council briefing material is circulated externally.

4.4 SDAB appeal pathway — relevant if Discretionary triggers expand

Constraint: The Subdivision and Development Appeal Board is chaired by Cllr. Candice Kolson, who also chairs Community Safety and Social Services. Kolson moved the successful amendment that retained semi-detached and cul-de-sac suites as Discretionary with 21-day notification and SDAB appeal — preserving neighbour input.

What this means operationally:

  • Any recommendation that adds Discretionary triggers (e.g., layering parcel-level Location Criteria onto the existing semi-detached / cul-de-sac triggers) lands at SDAB and is reviewed by a panel Kolson chairs.
  • The notification-and-appeal mechanism is something Kolson is publicly on record valuing. A recommendation framed in terms of strengthening Discretionary criteria (rather than retreating from Permitted Use designations) aligns with her preserved-input posture.
  • SDAB-appeal volume is a real administrative cost. The Red Deer Location Criteria operate at parcel level on Discretionary applications, so the SDAB load is finite and case-by-case rather than at neighbourhood scale.

Planner action item: If the recommended package includes a Tier 4 Location Criteria layer on the surviving Discretionary triggers, model the projected SDAB workload before recommending. Use the 2025 Discretionary-application volume as the baseline; assume a 10–20% appeal rate as a planning assumption for budget purposes (this is a planning assumption, not an empirical figure — verify against Airdrie's 2024–2025 SDAB caseload data).

5. Enforcement challenges

This is the operational equity finding the technical report carries forward from Stream F §2.0 and Red Team 3. It is the most important enforcement consideration in this file and the one most likely to produce a surprise at council if not surfaced now.

5.1 The underground suite risk

Finding (from Stream F §2.4 / SPUR 2006; technical report Methodology and Limitations): Restrictive regulations correlate with higher rates of unpermitted suites. Owners who cannot legalise a suite within the regulatory envelope often build or operate one anyway, off the books, removing the unit from health and safety oversight. This is the standard outcome documented across the U.S. and Canadian grey-literature record on suite regulation.

Implication for Airdrie: The two policy levers that suppress underground suites are (a) a permissive regulatory envelope (which B-30/2024 + B-06/2025 already deliver), and (b) a registration regime that makes legalisation easier than non-compliance. A concentration cap operates in the opposite direction — it makes legalisation harder for suites that would push a neighbourhood over the threshold, which is the population most likely to go underground.

5.2 Enforcement displacement — the top-line equity finding

Finding (Stream F §2.0, Red Team 3): A concentration cap is enforced primarily by removing non-compliant suites from the housing stock. In Airdrie's constrained rental market, enforcement displacement is a direct harm to existing tenants — disproportionately elderly and lower-income renters, and disproportionately the population the policy purports to protect.

This is not a Charter risk in the doctrinal sense. It is a primary harm of the regulatory mechanism and it lands on the people the framing invokes. The technical report carries this finding in the Methodology and Limitations section because it is the operational reality on which a Vavilov-reviewing court would assess reasonableness — and it is the operational reality council should not discover after passing a bylaw.

Implication for the briefing upward: Any recommendation that includes a cap-family mechanism must include an enforcement-displacement plan: notification protocols, transition windows, tenant protections, and an articulated equity rationale on the record. The absence of such a plan is itself a defensibility weakness.

5.3 Administrative cost of neighbourhood-level tracking

Finding (Stream A §4 / Red Deer ZB 3357/2024): A neighbourhood numerical cap requires neighbourhood-level tracking. Red Deer operates a publicly accessible web map tool showing each neighbourhood's current suite count relative to the 15% / 10% thresholds. This tool is the operational counterpart of the cap itself; without it, applicants cannot determine eligibility and staff cannot administer the bylaw consistently.

Implication for Airdrie: Adopting a cap-family mechanism requires an IT investment in a parallel tracking system, plus ongoing staff time to maintain it. The technical report does not quantify this cost for Airdrie (a planning assumption: similar cost magnitude to existing GIS / IM data products, plus integration with permit-tracking software). The CISG Standing Committee budget conversation should include this line item if the recommendation includes a cap.

5.4 Enforcement triage capacity

Finding (cross-cutting): Airdrie's bylaw services capacity is not surveyed in detail in the KT-018 streams, but Edmonton's UPE02476 disposition is the relevant comparator: Edmonton Administration recommended against tighter STR rules in 2025 citing "enforcement burden" as one of four reasons (alongside procedural-fairness, jurisdictional, and privacy concerns). Edmonton's STR-related nuisance complaints were 0.3% of total bylaw complaints; the operational case for high-friction STR enforcement was not made.

Implication for Airdrie: The STR baseline package recommended below (registration, principal-residence requirement, platform data-sharing, vacancy-trigger moratorium) is the lowest enforcement-cost lever Airdrie has — and one of the few that does not produce displacement of existing tenants. Calibrating STR enforcement around platform data-sharing and complaint-driven inspection is consistent with Edmonton's operational posture.

6. Options analysis

The four options below are ranked by defensibility against the four binding constraints (MGA s. 642, HAF, Charter/AHRA, equity). Each is scored on five dimensions to support an options-analysis brief to CISG.

Option 1 — Recommended package (registration + design standards + STR licence)

Mechanism set:
- STR business-licence bylaw with single-tier primary-residence licence, three-document principal-residence verification (Rocky View C-8640-2025 standard) modified per Red Team 3 equity guidance: plain-language application materials, alternative-evidence pathway for households with informal tenure.
- Sliding fee structure tied to gross STR revenue with micro-operator exemption (Stream E §11.1) — addresses Red Team 3 distributional concern.
- 180-night entire-home cap; no cap on bedroom-share within principal residence (Toronto standard).
- STR-in-suites contingency clause — Toronto/Halifax-style permission when suite is principal residence; outright prohibition held in reserve as contingency triggered by documented conversion above ~5% of new suites in 12 months or PBR vacancy below 2.5%.
- Platform data-sharing requirement modelled on Calgary's $3,000/yr platform fee + monthly listing submission.
- Vacancy-trigger moratorium clause modelled on Calgary's 2.5% PBR vacancy trigger.
- Built-form / design standards calibrated to manage operational neighbourhood-character impacts (privacy windows, transitions, garden suite siting rules, off-street parking placement) — not used to suppress supply.
- Secondary Suite Registry — Calgary-style catalogue at occupancy; HAF-reportable; addresses council's information asymmetry about where suites are.

Administrative burden Low Measurement-only; no new enforcement load
Political reception Low Council will likely want a substantive response to the concentration concern raised in the four-homes-per-lot consultation; pure measurement reads as evasion

Planner note: Option 4 is the inaction baseline. It is the right call if the empirical baseline for a cap-family recommendation is not yet in hand and council prefers measurement before mechanism. But it requires careful framing — "we are not yet acting because the evidence base for a defensible cap does not exist; we are committing to build that evidence base" — to avoid reading as evasion.

6.1 Recommended option

Option 1, with Option 2 as a conditional layer. The recommended path forward:

  • Bring Option 1 forward as the primary package — defensible across all four constraint dimensions, HAF-traceable, equity-calibrated, and aligned with Airdrie's existing regulatory architecture.
  • Bring Option 2 forward as a conditional add-on, available to council if the four-homes-per-lot consultation surfaces specific concentration concerns that cannot be addressed through built-form / design standards alone.
  • Park Option 3 unless a future evidentiary baseline supports it.
  • Frame Option 4 as the explicit fallback if council does not adopt Option 1 — to avoid the implicit "no action" default.

7. How to brief upward

7.1 Briefing the Community Infrastructure and Strategic Growth Standing Committee (Cllr. Ron Chapman, chair)

CISG is the planning committee that vets land use and growth files before they reach full council. Chapman is the top vote-getter on council (5,834 votes), suggesting strong public mandate, and chairs the planning pipeline. Suggested briefing structure (60-minute slot):

  1. Open with the bottom line (5 min): Concentration caps on suites are not a common Canadian practice and are legally vulnerable; Airdrie has a more defensible path through registration, STR licensing, and design standards. The Cochrane cap-removal precedent and Calgary's repeal-with-amendment manoeuvre are the closest peer references.
  2. HAF traceability (15 min): Walk through the four-tier HAF taxonomy. Map each recommended Option 1 mechanism against the HAF Action Plan commitments. Show that Option 1 does not require the two-key amendment pathway under Bill 18.
  3. MGA s. 642 mechanic (10 min): Plainly explain the Permitted Use ratchet and what it means for the remaining levers. Avoid jargon. The surviving Discretionary triggers (semi-detached, cul-de-sac) are the planner's natural insertion points for any Location Criteria layer.
  4. Edmonton 50.4% data (10 min): The single most useful data point for swing-vote councillors. Of 242 eight-unit-eligible permits in 2024, only 122 (50.4%) chose maximum density. Reframe the political conversation from "if we allow more, we get maxed-out everywhere" to "the market chooses heterogeneity even when given full unlock."
  5. Cochrane 232% data (5 min): Cap-removal precedent. Releasing concentration constraints produced a 232% application increase in 2024 with no documented organised backlash. This is the cleanest available Alberta evidence.
  6. Options analysis (10 min): Present the four ranked options with the scoring table. Recommend Option 1 with Option 2 as conditional layer.
  7. Q&A and next steps (5 min): Identify which committee members want followups. Confirm the bylaw drafting timeline aligns with the four-homes-per-lot bylaw deliberation window.

7.2 Addressing the swing-vote new councillors (Glass, Shima, Obasan, Stewart)

These four councillors have no public record on suite density or STRs. The neighbourhood-character concern is the most likely lens through which they will read the file. The Edmonton 50.4% data is the single highest-leverage data point for this audience. It directly addresses the "if we permit more, every yard becomes a maxed-out site" intuition without invoking ideological framings.

Secondary framing:

  • Stewart (chairs Corporate Services): Lead with the budget impact. Option 1 has medium administrative burden; Option 3 has high administrative burden plus neighbourhood-level tracking IT investment. Quantify the operational difference.
  • Glass (Airdrie Housing Limited Board): Lead with the HAF compliance. Glass's AHL role makes him receptive to the supply-protection rationale; the STR licence's "long-term rental stock protection" framing should land cleanly.
  • Shima and Obasan: Lead with the empirical evidence. The 50.4% Edmonton data and 232% Cochrane data answer the "but what if" concerns without requiring ideological commitment.

7.3 Framing HAF constraints without alarming council

Mayor Spearman previously expressed wanting "more clarity around the city's Housing Accelerator Fund" before allocating additional funds. The HAF context for this file is real (a $24.8M agreement, 39% of milestones complete, and $12.4M received on the public record available to the dossier), but it should be framed as a navigable constraint, not a paralysing one. Confirm current disbursement status before publication.

Three framing principles:

  1. Lead with the path Airdrie can take, not the path Red Deer took. The Red Deer cancellation is a useful comparator but should not be the opening frame. Calgary's repeal-with-amendment manoeuvre demonstrates that HAF compliance can survive a substantive policy adjustment — the HAF target is unit count, not unit typology.
  2. Show the four-tier HAF taxonomy upfront. Council can read the taxonomy and understand which mechanisms are HAF-safe (Tier 1 / Tier 4) and which carry HAF risk (Tier 2 / Tier 3). This converts the HAF discussion from abstract to operational.
  3. Be explicit about the two-key Bill 18 pathway. Council should know that any HAF Action Plan amendment now travels through Alberta Cabinet under the Provincial Priorities Act. This is not an argument against acting — it is an argument for choosing the package (Option 1) that does not require an Action Plan amendment.

7.4 Addressing the neighbourhood-character concern

The neighbourhood-character receptor is the standard frame for opposition. The technical report's Methodology and Limitations section flags that empirical validation of neighbourhood-character claims is methodologically difficult; complaint-density data is the proxy but suffers from selection bias.

Two operational responses are available:

  1. Built-form / design standards. A robust design standards layer (privacy windows, transitions, garden suite siting rules, off-street parking placement, screening, materials) addresses the substantive operational concerns that "neighbourhood character" usually denotes — without requiring a cap-family mechanism. This is the most defensible response.
  2. The Edmonton 50.4% data. Where the concern is "if we permit more, every yard maxes out," the data answers directly.

Where the concern is articulated as opposition to renters as a class (overlapping with low-income, single-parent, newcomer occupancy), the planner should flag the user-vs-use doctrine (Stream F §1.2) and the AHRA s. 5 housing-provision protection (Stream F §2.3) internally before any council-facing material is finalised. A recommendation that responds to such opposition with a cap is doctrinally vulnerable.

7.5 Addressing Cllr. Kolson's neighbourhood-input concern

Cllr. Kolson chairs SDAB and moved the amendment that retained semi-detached and cul-de-sac suites as Discretionary with 21-day notification. The Option 1 package preserves notification mechanisms (registration carries a documentation pathway; STR licensing carries a complaint-driven inspection model) without requiring discretionary refusal of Permitted-Use applications.

If the recommendation includes Option 2 (parcel-level Location Criteria on existing Discretionary triggers), the framing is directly aligned with Kolson's preserved-input posture: the Criteria strengthen the notification-and-appeal pathway she already values. The SDAB workload projection should be modelled and shared with her chair role.

7.6 What to NOT do at the council briefing

  • Do not frame Airdrie as "behind." Spearman is unlikely to lead with a defensive framing; the dossier is most useful as forward-looking strategic positioning. The framing is "evidence-based first-time regime drawn from regional best practice," not "we should have done this years ago."
  • Do not overstate the supply-protection threat. The McGill / StatsCan numbers (16,000 BC homes; 19% decline; 28% rent rise; 31,000 nationally) apply to tight-vacancy, high-tourism markets. Airdrie's proportional gain is "tens of units, not hundreds" (Stream E §11.4). Overstating the threat is a credibility risk if industry voices push back during public engagement.
  • Do not present concentration caps as a defensible default. They are not common Canadian practice and are vulnerable on three doctrinal axes. A planner who presents a cap as the natural answer to a concentration concern will be challenged on the comparative scan and on the Charter/AHRA exposure.
  • Do not name the Planning Director / GM until internal confirmation. The Landscape Scan flagged this gap (§4.3 above). Verify through internal channels before any external material attributes file ownership.
