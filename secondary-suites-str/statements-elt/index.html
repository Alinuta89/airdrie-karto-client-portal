Executive Leadership Briefing — Secondary Suite Density and STR Controls

References: kt-018/deliverables/technical-report.md; kt-018/deliverables/decision-support-brief.md

1. Organizational risk summary (30-second read)

  • The policy question. Council is considering whether to introduce a geographic or neighbourhood-level cap on secondary suite concentration, and how to position short-term rental (STR) regulation alongside it. Administration is asked to advise on the organizational implications before council direction is set.
  • The HAF exposure. Airdrie's $24.8M Housing Accelerator Fund (HAF) Contribution Agreement is a binding commitment to expand housing supply. Any new mechanism that materially constrains agreed supply targets — including a numerical concentration cap on secondary suites — places at minimum the remaining $12.4M in instalments at risk, with the documented federal remedy being instalment-pause first and termination only if uncured. The Red Deer cancellation (January 16, 2026) is the controlling precedent for what termination looks like.
  • The recommended organizational position. Adopt the STR business-licence framework now (HAF-safe, operationally beneficial, low resource intensity); commit to a 12-month measurement baseline before any concentration-control mechanism is considered; preserve organizational optionality on a future Red Deer-style empirically calibrated mechanism if council directs it. Do not adopt a numerical cap absent Airdrie-specific evidentiary calibration.

The balance of this briefing develops the rationale and the organizational governance issues ELT should hold before council deliberation.

2. HAF contractual exposure — the binding constraint

The HAF Contribution Agreement is the single largest factor in the organizational risk calculus. The exposure is not theoretical and the remedy gradient is now documented.

Agreement status. Airdrie signed the $24.8M HAF Contribution Agreement in 2024 against seven commitments, including streamlining the Land Use Bylaw to allow four units as-of-right and reducing parking and lot-size barriers for secondary suites. CMHC's public Action Plan Summary lists total estimated eligibility of $24.839M, structured as four annual advances of $6.20975M. Local reporting in the dossier states that, as of the October 2024 council update, $12.4M had been received and 39% of milestones were complete. Because this briefing is dated 2026-05-11 and the next advance was expected in March 2026 on the public record available to the dossier, current disbursement status should be confirmed with the City or CMHC before council-facing use.

The four-tier HAF risk taxonomy. Stream F's legal analysis identifies four tiers of HAF exposure that ELT should hold in mind when reading any cap-family recommendation:

Tier Mechanism Risk profile Precedent
1 Refusal of a mandated expansion commitment Termination Red Deer (January 16, 2026)
2 Net-new post-HAF cap that materially constrains agreed supply targets Instalment-pause; termination if uncured Calgary (February–April 2026)
3 Cap that pre-dated HAF signing Not inherently a trigger Red Deer pre-HAF posture
4 Post-HAF discretionary proximity tool that does not constrain supply targets Not a trigger on available evidence Red Deer Location Criteria

Where Airdrie sits. Airdrie did not have a cap at HAF signing, so Tier 3 does not apply. Any cap considered now is by definition post-HAF — Tier 2 if numerical and supply-constraining, Tier 4 if structured as parcel-level discretionary proximity at the development-permit stage. Tier 2 risk is real and documented in the Calgary letter; the available remedy is instalment-pause first, not litigation. A council reader unfamiliar with the gradient may infer that any density-controlling mechanism puts HAF at risk; the more precise reading is that the trigger is supply-constraint, not the existence of a tool.

The Red Deer precedent — what termination looks like. On December 19, 2025, CMHC issued Red Deer a 21-day breach notice; the agreement was cancelled on January 16, 2026. Red Deer Council voted 7–2 to retain the $3M first advance and pursue dialogue with Ottawa. The remaining $9M in instalments is lost; the City carries ongoing exposure to a federal repayment claim. The trigger was Council's July 2025 refusal to adopt four-units-as-of-right — not the City's pre-existing 15% neighbourhood numerical cap (Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 §4.50.5, empirically calibrated to a 9-of-61 / 2-of-61 distribution) or its parcel-level Location Criteria. Both pre-HAF instruments coexisted with the agreement at signing and at cancellation.

The Calgary precedent — what partial non-compliance looks like. On February 10, 2026, CMHC wrote to Calgary City Council stating compliance requires the City to "allow for at least four units on a lot across the city without additional approvals, and must not reintroduce approval processes or other barriers that slow down development." Approximately $861M was reported at risk. After the citywide rezoning repeal on April 8, 2026, the third instalment ($64.7M) was conditionally approved; the fourth (~$60M) remains conditional on a replacement plan in effect or significantly developed by October 27, 2026.

Implication for Airdrie. CMHC actively monitors density-reducing bylaw movements by HAF signatories, pauses or withholds instalments rather than relying on post-hoc clawback litigation, and looks for functional equivalence to the original commitment rather than to the original wording. A cap mechanism that achieves the same supply-suppressing end as a repeal of four-units-as-of-right draws the same response.

The PPA two-key approval pathway. Since April 1, 2025, the Provincial Priorities Act (Bill 18) requires Alberta entities — including municipalities — to obtain provincial approval before entering into, amending, extending, or renewing an intergovernmental agreement. The implementing regulation (Provincial Priorities Regulation, Alta. Reg. 21/2025) sets thresholds: $100,000 generally; $250,000 for housing accommodations. Airdrie's $24.8M agreement is far above both thresholds and above the $5M Cabinet threshold. Any negotiated retreat from HAF commitments — whether to add a cap, narrow four-units-as-of-right, or otherwise reduce the supply commitment — now requires both CMHC consent and Alberta Cabinet approval. This is a two-veto structure: either side can independently block the change. Provincial cover may be more readily available than at any earlier point given the Premier's and Minister Nixon's public posture in defence of Red Deer, but federal approval would simultaneously become harder. The pathway is multi-month and multi-stakeholder with no guarantee of either approval.

Bottom line on HAF exposure. Airdrie is not in Red Deer's position. The organizational priority is to stay out of it. A numerical concentration cap added now is the mechanism most likely to put HAF at risk; the cap-adjacent mechanisms that minimize HAF exposure (parcel-level discretionary proximity at the permit stage, registration-based regulation, design and form standards) are documented in §4.6 of the technical report and §5 of the decision-support brief.

3. Legal liability exposure — Charter, AHRA, and the displacement frame

Charter and provincial human-rights exposure under a secondary-suite cap is not theoretical, and ELT should not let the doctrinal complexity obscure the operational point: enforcement of a concentration cap removes suites from the housing stock, and the displaced tenant is the organization's exposure surface.

The operational harm precedes the doctrinal exposure. Enforcement against non-compliant suites under a cap bylaw displaces existing tenants in a constrained rental market. The displaced tenant does not return to the regulated market on the same terms — re-housing into the formal multi-family market typically occurs at higher rents and reduced security of tenure. The affordability protection a cap is framed to deliver works against the renter the framing invokes. This is the top-line equity finding in Stream F (§2.0) and it should be acknowledged on the record before any cap-family mechanism is adopted. A council that adopts a cap without acknowledging the displacement mechanic carries a more exposed Vavilov reasonableness record than a council that demonstrably balanced the harm.

Charter section 15 — adverse-effect exposure. Secondary suites are disproportionately occupied by groups overlapping with Charter-protected characteristics: low-income tenants, single parents (family status), newcomers, students, persons with disabilities living in accessible units, and seniors aging in place. The leading Canadian authority on neighbourhood-level housing-distribution rules is Alcoholism Foundation of Manitoba v. Winnipeg (Manitoba Court of Appeal), which held that a zoning bylaw breached Charter s. 15 by restricting group homes to a limited number of zones with separation distances. No reported Canadian case has directly adjudicated a suite concentration cap; the Alcoholism Foundation line is the best available analogue. A facially neutral cap survives the Bell v. The Queen user-vs-use test but remains exposed to an adverse-effect claim where it disproportionately constrains a protected group.

Alberta Human Rights Act — a parallel exposure pathway, not a subsidiary one. The AHRA protects, relevantly: family status, source of income (under the housing-provision protected grounds, s. 5), and disability. A concentration cap that reduces the supply of secondary suites carries AHRA s. 5 exposure independent of the Charter analysis — the AHRA pathway does not require state action in the Charter sense, and the Alberta Human Rights Commission's investigative function is less deferential than judicial reasonableness review. Specifically:

  • Family status. Secondary suites disproportionately house single-parent families, multigenerational households, and intergenerational care configurations. A cap reduces the supply of the housing form best suited to these tenure structures.
  • Source of income. Lower-income tenants — including households receiving income assistance, AISH, seniors benefits, or minimum-wage incomes — are over-represented in secondary-suite tenancy because suites are the most affordable rental form in suburban Alberta.
  • Disability. Garden, garage, and ground-level internal suites are an accessibility tenure form for persons with mobility limitations who cannot navigate multi-storey housing. Reducing the supply of accessible suites operates as adverse-effect discrimination on disability grounds.

Why this matters for ELT. A cap bylaw that survives Charter reasonableness review can still be the subject of an AHRA adverse-effect complaint by an affected family, lower-income tenant, or person with a disability. The Commission's complaint pathway is more accessible than a judicial review application and the remedy can include orders to amend the offending provision and compensation. The AHRA pathway is a second front; the package should not treat it as subsumed within the Charter analysis.

Reasonableness-review exposure. A municipal bylaw is reviewable on the Vavilov reasonableness standard. Vavilov requires the reviewing court to look at the record as a whole, and "a lack of responsive reasons can be a stand-alone basis for quashing a decision" (Lerners LLP). A cap with no evidentiary record explaining why this percentage or why this distance is more vulnerable than a cap supported by parking studies, nuisance-complaint data, infrastructure capacity analysis, or the kind of empirical neighbourhood-by-neighbourhood distribution Red Deer recorded. Airdrie does not currently hold the Airdrie-specific empirical record that would calibrate a cap to the Vavilov standard. Adopting one before the evidence exists is the legally exposed position.

Acknowledged equity gap. The KT-018 package does not address Indigenous housing rights or newcomer household vulnerability as distinct equity considerations. Engagement with the Airdrie Local Immigration Partnership and with regional Indigenous housing organizations (including Aboriginal Friendship Centre of Calgary, Métis Nation of Alberta Region 3 housing portfolio, and Tsuut'ina / Stoney Nakoda housing liaison channels) is a precondition for any cap-family mechanism, not a post-hoc consultation. The HAF-safe baseline package (registration + design standards + STR business licence) carries a lower engagement bar because it expands rather than constrains supply.

4. Implementation complexity and resource implications

A numerical neighbourhood cap is not a one-time bylaw amendment. It is a sustained administrative commitment that the organization must size honestly before recommending council adopt it.

What a numerical cap actually requires. Operating a Red Deer-style 15% neighbourhood cap calls for:

  • Neighbourhood-level tracking infrastructure. Red Deer operates a public Web Map tool that shows neighbourhood-by-neighbourhood suite distribution and the gap to the cap. Building and maintaining the equivalent for Airdrie is a GIS and planning-data commitment, not a bylaw drafting task.
  • Intake-stage verification. Every development-permit application for a secondary suite must be checked against the neighbourhood's current suite count and the cap. This adds a verification step to a permit category that HAF expects to be streamlined.
  • Ongoing data maintenance. Suites are added, removed, converted to STR, decommissioned, and re-registered. The neighbourhood count must be maintained continuously, not as an annual snapshot, or the cap becomes unenforceable.
  • Discretionary-application defensibility. If the cap operates through the Discretionary Use mechanic (as Red Deer's Location Criteria do), every refused application is appealable to the Subdivision and Development Appeal Board. The City must defend each refusal on the record. This is a planning-capacity load.
  • Bylaw-enforcement exposure on the back end. Enforcement against suites that exceed the cap is the mechanism that produces displacement (§3 above). Under-resourcing enforcement creates the Toronto Auditor General-style risk profile; over-resourcing it produces the displacement-on-enforcement outcome. There is no resourcing level at which a cap bylaw is administratively neutral.

What a registration / business-licence framework requires. Registration applies at the point of operation (not at the point of land-use approval), uses a published rate-sheet, and is enforced by complaint with periodic inspection. The City already operates business-licence and building-permit pipelines that handle adjacent functions. Marginal organizational lift is comparable to the existing STR-adjacent licensing workload, not a new program build.

Implementation conclusion. Registration delivers most of the operational benefits ELT and council are looking for — operator accountability, fire and life-safety oversight, complaint resolution, revenue — at materially lower administrative cost than a cap. A cap is not a substitute for registration; it adds an administrative layer on top of one. If the organization chooses to build only one of the two pipelines in the current cycle, registration is the higher-yield choice.

5. Strategic fit with current GOA direction

Alberta has not enacted a Housing Statutes (Residential Development) Amendment Act (BC Bill 44) or More Homes Built Faster Act (Ontario Bill 23) analogue. Municipalities retain full Land Use Bylaw authority over secondary suite definition, density, parking, separation, and lot-size requirements, subject to the Municipal Government Act. Airdrie has more local discretion than its BC or Ontario peers.

But local discretion runs in both directions. Airdrie also has less provincial cover when council faces neighbourhood pushback than a BC or Ontario municipality would. In BC, a municipality can — and West Vancouver did — be pressed by the province to adopt density zoning consistent with Bill 44; the provincial direction substitutes for local political will. Alberta does not provide that backstop. A council facing neighbourhood pressure for a cap cannot point to provincial direction as a reason it cannot do so; it must defend the decision on its own record.

Current GOA signals. The province is publicly pro-supply on the housing file. The Premier and Minister Nixon's defence of Red Deer is framed as preserving municipal flexibility against federal overreach, not as encouraging caps. The Bill 18 / PPA architecture functions as a procedural lever that the province can deploy in either direction. A cap adopted without an evidentiary record would be inconsistent with the provincial pro-supply posture even where it is not provincially prohibited.

Strategic implication. Airdrie's strategic position is strongest when it is visibly pro-supply in a way that preserves local control over operational impacts (registration, design standards, parking calibration, STR licensing). The strategic position is weakest when council adopts a supply-constraining mechanism that simultaneously (a) puts the federal HAF agreement at risk, (b) cannot be defended on a Vavilov record, and (c) does not align with provincial direction. The current package frames a path that maintains the strong position.

6. Staff accountability gap — internal governance issue

This is an internal governance issue independent of the policy decision, but ELT should resolve it before the file proceeds to council.

The observed gap. A scan of publicly available Airdrie sources (airdrie.ca, council agendas, council package attribution lines) does not identify a Planning Director or equivalent senior planning lead by name as the file owner for the secondary-suite density and STR regulation portfolio. The Land Use Planning service area is operational on the airdrie.ca site, and Planning and Development staff are referenced as the author body for prior bylaw amendments — but no single director-level accountability is publicly visible for this specific file.

Why this matters. Before any bylaw amendment that engages a $24.8M federal Contribution Agreement proceeds to council, the organization should confirm — internally and on the record — who holds accountability for:

  1. The HAF compliance review. Has the file lead reviewed the proposed mechanism against the four-tier HAF taxonomy and signed off that the recommended path does not engage Tier 1 or Tier 2 exposure?
  2. The Alberta human rights review. Has the file lead obtained the City Solicitor's opinion on AHRA s. 5 exposure under the recommended package?
  3. The Vavilov record review. Is the evidentiary record that would support the bylaw amendment on a reasonableness-review standard assembled and on file? If not, what is the plan to assemble it before second reading?
  4. The PPA pre-clearance review. If the recommended path includes any element that would amend the HAF Contribution Agreement, has the Alberta Cabinet approval pathway been pre-scoped with Municipal Affairs?

Recommended internal action. Before the May 12 Committee of the Whole, ELT should:

  • Confirm the file lead at director level and brief them on the HAF / AHRA / Vavilov / PPA review points above.
  • Confirm the City Solicitor's review schedule for any contemplated bylaw amendment.
  • Confirm that the council package includes a HAF compliance line — not as a separate report, but as a standing line in the staff report template for any bylaw amendment touching housing supply.

This is a governance recommendation, not a policy recommendation. It applies regardless of which substantive path council directs.

7. Recommended path — three horizons

The recommended path is structured across three horizons to preserve organizational optionality while delivering near-term operational benefit and building the evidentiary base for any future concentration-control mechanism.

Horizon 1 — Immediate (next 60 days)

Adopt the STR business-licence framework. Modelled on Calgary's two-tier Primary / Non-Primary licence architecture, with:

  • Operator registration and annual licensing fee
  • Distinction between primary-residence (host-occupied) and non-primary (investor) operations
  • Night-cap on non-primary operations calibrated to local conditions
  • Vacancy-trigger pause on net-new non-primary licences (Calgary's mechanism at Business Licence Bylaw 32M98 § 58.1(3.2), prospective-only, objectively triggered by published CMHC vacancy rate)
  • Procedural-fairness compliance for revocation (notice, opportunity to respond, written reasons, appeal pathway — Vancouver-style)
  • Enforcement resourcing sized to the regulated population at the outset, not after the Toronto Auditor General-style audit

Why now. This package is HAF-safe (Tier 4 at most), administratively contained, and delivers measurable benefit on the operational concerns council currently hears about secondary-suite use. It also generates licence-fee revenue that partially funds the broader compliance program. No HAF Contribution Agreement amendment is required.

Horizon 2 — Short-term (12 months)

Commission the Airdrie-specific evidentiary baseline. Three data products:

  • Secondary-suite concentration map. Neighbourhood-by-neighbourhood share of registered suites against total dwelling units. Airdrie-specific equivalent of the Red Deer 9-of-61 / 2-of-61 distribution analysis.
  • Complaint-volume mapping. Geocoded bylaw and Service Airdrie complaint data tagged to suite-related categories (parking, noise, occupancy, property maintenance) over a 24–36 month look-back.
  • Parking saturation and infrastructure indicators. On-street parking utilization in older / narrower-frontage neighbourhoods; servicing capacity flags; school-catchment utilization where relevant.

Why this matters. This is the record that would support a Vavilov-reasonable concentration-control mechanism if council later directs one. Without it, any cap is exposed to the reasonableness-review risk identified in §3 above. Building the record before the political moment requires it preserves council's optionality and reduces organizational litigation exposure.

Resource estimate. The data products are within existing GIS and Planning Services capacity, supplemented by complaint-data extraction from the bylaw enforcement system. Not a new program; an analytic deliverable.

Horizon 3 — Medium-term (12–24 months)

Revisit concentration tools once the data exists. If the Horizon 2 baseline shows neighbourhood-level concentration patterns that warrant a concentration-control mechanism, the recommended template is the Red Deer architecture as reframed in Stream F §4.6:

  • A numerical neighbourhood cap calibrated to the observed distribution (not to a round-number policy preference)
  • Paired with parcel-level discretionary proximity considerations applied at the development-permit stage (Tier 4 in the HAF taxonomy)
  • Adopted with a documented evidentiary record on the planning rationale (parking, fire access, servicing capacity, or nuisance-grounded — not "neighbourhood character" alone)
  • Accompanied by the Indigenous and newcomer-service engagement record identified as a precondition

If the Horizon 2 baseline does not show such patterns, the registration-and-design-standards package adopted at Horizon 1 is the complete answer and no concentration mechanism is recommended.

Either outcome is defensible. The organization is not committed to a cap by adopting this path; it is committed to the evidence on which a cap would or would not be justified.

What the three horizons deliver, organizationally

  • HAF compliance preserved. No mechanism in Horizons 1 or 2 engages Tier 2 risk.
  • Vavilov record built. Horizon 2 produces the empirical calibration that any future cap requires.
  • AHRA exposure managed. The Horizon 1 package expands supply through registration; the Horizon 3 conditional reopens concentration only if the evidentiary record supports it.
  • Council optionality preserved. Council is not closed out of a cap; it is asked to base one on Airdrie evidence rather than on the Red Deer template imported without local calibration.
  • Organizational governance gap closed. Horizon 1 forces resolution of the file-lead accountability question before any bylaw amendment proceeds.

8. What ELT should flag to council before May 12

Two non-negotiable organizational positions should be flagged to council before the Committee of the Whole, regardless of which policy path council subsequently directs.

Position 1 — No concentration mechanism without Airdrie-specific data. A numerical or geographic concentration cap on secondary suites adopted now, without an Airdrie-specific evidentiary record on neighbourhood distribution, complaint volumes, and infrastructure capacity, would:

  • Be exposed to Vavilov reasonableness-review challenge with a thin record
  • Engage Tier 2 HAF risk under the four-tier taxonomy
  • Carry AHRA s. 5 adverse-effect exposure on family-status, source-of-income, and disability grounds
  • Produce displacement-on-enforcement outcomes that fall on the renters the policy purports to protect
  • Require a sustained administrative commitment that Airdrie is not currently sized to deliver

The organizational position is that a concentration mechanism, if council determines one is warranted, should be considered at Horizon 3 of the recommended path — after the Horizon 2 baseline is in place. This is the position the Red Deer record supports (their 15% threshold was calibrated to observed distribution, not adopted in advance of it) and the position the Vavilov / AHRA framework requires.

Position 2 — Any HAF Contribution Agreement amendment is a two-key process. Since April 1, 2025, any negotiated change to Airdrie's $24.8M HAF Contribution Agreement requires:

  • Federal renegotiation with CMHC. Subject to the Calgary functional-equivalence standard (CMHC seeks restoration of the original supply outcome, not preservation of the original wording) and the Red Deer termination precedent.
  • Alberta Cabinet approval under Bill 18 / Alta. Reg. 21/2025. Above the $5M Cabinet threshold; the housing-accommodation $250,000 exemption does not rescue an amendment of a $24.8M pre-existing agreement.

The two-key structure means either side can independently block the change. Provincial cover may be more readily available than at any earlier point given the Premier's and Minister Nixon's defence of Red Deer, but federal approval would simultaneously become harder. The pathway is multi-month and multi-stakeholder. Council should not assume that a future amendment is procedurally available simply because the original agreement was negotiated quickly in 2024.

This is the position the procedural record supports, and it applies regardless of substantive policy direction.

9. Decision points for the May 12 Committee of the Whole

ELT recommends that the staff report to Committee of the Whole include the following decision points framed for council direction, in order:

  1. Direction on the STR business-licence framework. Direct administration to prepare a draft business-licence bylaw amendment for STR regulation, modelled on the Calgary two-tier architecture with Vancouver-style procedural-fairness compliance, for first reading at a subsequent regular council meeting.
  2. Direction on the evidentiary baseline. Direct administration to commission the three-product data baseline (concentration map, complaint mapping, parking and infrastructure indicators) and to report back within 12 months.
  3. Direction on concentration tools. Confirm that no concentration-control mechanism on secondary suites will be advanced for adoption ahead of the evidentiary baseline, and that any subsequent consideration will be paired with the Indigenous and newcomer-service engagement record identified as a precondition.
  4. Direction on HAF compliance reporting. Direct administration to include a HAF compliance line as a standing element in staff reports for any bylaw amendment touching housing supply, and to provide a closed-session briefing to ELT and council on the four-tier HAF taxonomy and the PPA two-key pathway before any contemplated Contribution Agreement amendment is advanced.

The decision-support brief (kt-018/deliverables/decision-support-brief.md) elaborates the policy reasoning behind each direction; the technical report (kt-018/deliverables/technical-report.md) provides the full jurisdictional scan and citation record.

10. What this briefing does not address

The KT-018 package and this briefing do not provide:

  • A draft bylaw. Drafting follows council direction at Committee of the Whole and is a separate scope of work.
  • A City Solicitor opinion. The legal-defensibility analysis in Stream F is research-grade, not litigation-grade. Before any bylaw amendment reaches second reading, the City Solicitor's opinion should be obtained — particularly on AHRA s. 5 exposure and on the Alberta-specific Vavilov record.
  • The unredacted HAF Contribution Agreement and Action Plan text. Stream F worked from the publicly summarized seven commitments; the granular wording of "barriers" and "approval processes" matters for risk calibration on any subsequent mechanism. The unredacted Action Plan should be requested from the City Manager's Office before any cap-adjacent recommendation reaches council.
  • The City Solicitor's application advice on the Provincial Priorities Regulation. Stream F's reading of Alta. Reg. 21/2025 is grounded in the public regulation text via CanLII, the Alberta Municipalities summary, and the RMA legal backgrounder. Before any council-facing recommendation that turns on the PPA threshold, obtain a City Solicitor opinion on its application to amendments of pre-existing above-threshold agreements.
  • Indigenous and newcomer-service engagement. Acknowledged as a precondition for any cap-family mechanism (§3 above); not closed within the KT-018 timeline.

These gaps are noted on the record so that the May 12 staff report can flag them for council without overstating the package's completeness.
