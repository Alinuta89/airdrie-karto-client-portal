Decision-Support Brief — Secondary Suite Density & STR Controls

City of Airdrie | KT-018 | 2026-05-11
Prepared by: Karto Research (HelpSeeker Technologies)
Purpose: Decision-ready, standalone reference on whether to adopt geographic concentration caps on secondary suites, and on the defensible alternatives that could achieve council's underlying density-management objectives without putting the City's $24.8M Housing Accelerator Fund (HAF) agreement at risk.
Read alongside: the full technical report (jurisdictional scan of 20 municipalities, legal defensibility analysis, evidence record).

A. Direct Answer — Are geographic concentration caps on secondary suites commonly used, and are they legally defensible?

Uncommon, conditionally defensible, and HAF-risky in Airdrie's specific situation.

Across the 20 Canadian municipalities scanned, Red Deer is the only Alberta peer using a numerical neighbourhood concentration cap for secondary suites — a 15% cap on the share of detached dwellings that may contain a House Suite or Backyard Suite, layered with parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria (proximity, design, accessibility, parking), under Zoning Bylaw 3357/2024 §4.50.5. No other Alberta peer (Calgary, Edmonton, Lethbridge, Medicine Hat, St. Albert, Cochrane, Okotoks) operates a numerical cap on suites. Among large urban comparators, Vancouver, Toronto, Mississauga, Saskatoon, Ottawa, Kelowna, Kitchener, and Victoria operate without a numerical concentration cap; some use parking, lot-size, design, or operational tools instead.

Caps can be legally defensible — Red Deer is the available Canadian model — but only when they meet the Catalyst Paper / Vavilov reasonableness standard: empirical calibration, an evidentiary record explaining the chosen threshold, and a mechanism that regulates use intensity rather than user identity (the Bell / Alcoholism Foundation line of authority). Red Deer's 15% cap was calibrated to a documented baseline: 9 of 61 neighbourhoods exceeded 10% and only 2 (South Hill and West Park east) exceeded 15% at the time of adoption. Airdrie has no equivalent baseline measurement on the public record, which is a material defensibility gap if the City were to adopt a cap today.

The harder constraint is contractual, not legal. Airdrie signed a $24.8M HAF Contribution Agreement in 2024, committing to four-units-as-of-right, parking reductions, and a secondary-suite incentive program. CMHC's public Action Plan Summary lists total estimated eligibility of $24.839M, structured as four annual advances of $6.20975M. Local reporting in the dossier states that, as of late 2025, $12.4M had been received and 39% of milestones were complete; the next advance was expected in March 2026. Because this brief is dated 2026-05-11, the current disbursement status must be confirmed with the City or CMHC before client-facing publication. CMHC has demonstrated through the Calgary letter (Feb 10, 2026) and the Red Deer termination (effective Jan 16, 2026) that it will enforce supply commitments. The four-tier HAF risk taxonomy below identifies which cap-family mechanisms are HAF-safe and which are not.

HAF four-tier risk taxonomy (controlling for Airdrie):

Tier Mechanism HAF status Available to Airdrie?
1 Refusing a mandated expansion commitment (e.g., refusing four-units-as-of-right) Termination trigger — confirmed (Red Deer, 2026-01-16) No — do not.
2 Net-new post-HAF cap that materially constrains agreed supply targets Instalment-pause / conditioning trigger — confirmed (Calgary, Feb–Apr 2026) High risk; not recommended without CMHC pre-consultation
3 Cap pre-existing at HAF signing Not inherently a trigger (CMHC signed Red Deer's HAF-2 with the 15% cap already on the books) N/A — Airdrie had no cap at signing
4 Discretionary tools applied post-HAF that do not constrain supply targets at the parcel level (e.g., proximity considerations at the development-permit stage) Not a trigger on available evidence Available — lowest-risk cap-adjacent option

The short version: a numerical neighbourhood cap on Airdrie's books today would land in Tier 2 and is the highest-risk HAF surface short of outright refusal. A Red-Deer-style parcel-level discretionary mechanism (Tier 4) is the cap-adjacent option with the lowest HAF risk on the current record.

B. Key Risks of Implementing a Cap

1. Legal risk. Under Vavilov and Catalyst Paper, a council adopting a concentration cap must produce a record explaining why the chosen threshold is calibrated to a measurable concern. Airdrie has no public baseline of suite distribution, no complaint-volume data, no parking-saturation measurement, and no rental-stock-conversion data. A cap adopted without that record is exposed to reasonableness-review challenge. Under s. 15 of the Charter and the Alberta Human Rights Act (AHRA), concentration caps have credible adverse-effect exposure on protected grounds (family status, source of income, disability) — particularly where the cap restricts the housing forms most accessible to lower-income, single-parent, newcomer, and disabled renters. The AHRA pathway runs alongside the Charter analysis, not behind it, and is independently actionable.

2. HAF contractual risk. A net-new cap added now would constitute a Tier 2 mechanism under the four-tier taxonomy and would expose Airdrie to the same instalment-conditioning treatment CMHC applied to Calgary. Airdrie's next HAF advance was expected in March 2026 on the public record available to this dossier; confirm whether it has been released, delayed, or conditioned before presenting timing risk to council.

3. Procedural / provincial risk. Any negotiated amendment of Airdrie's HAF Action Plan (e.g., to add density-controlling mechanisms) is now reviewable under Alberta's Provincial Priorities Act (in force 2025-04-01) and the Provincial Priorities Regulation (Alta. Reg. 21/2025). Because Airdrie's agreement exceeds $5M, amendment approval lies with Alberta Cabinet, not the Minister alone. This is a two-key approval pathway (CMHC consent + Cabinet approval), each of which can independently block the change. The minor-administrative-amendment carve-out does not cover substantive Action Plan changes.

4. Administrative and equity risk. Cap enforcement is itself a displacement mechanism. Identifying and removing existing tenants from "over-cap" suites — which is the operational end-state of enforcing a hard concentration cap — disproportionately falls on lower-income, single-parent, newcomer, and elderly renters who occupy the very housing the cap is designed to constrain. This is the package's top-line equity finding. Caps also drive underground suite creation (a one-year-Cochrane signal of 232% application growth after cap-removal is in the record; the inverse risk on cap-adoption is documented across jurisdictions). Appeals to the Subdivision and Development Appeal Board (SDAB) are likely under all cap-family designs.

5. Evidentiary risk. Beyond Vavilov exposure, the absence of an Airdrie-specific local-harm record means council would be regulating in advance of measurement. Both senior municipal advisors and industry counsel would predictably attack the package on this ground. Stress test residual R1 applies: any cap-family recommendation should be framed as conditional on a 12-month Airdrie measurement program (complaint volume, parking-saturation observation, STR registration uptake, rental-stock-conversion data), with Phase 2 calibration following the measurement data.

C. Viable Policy Alternatives Used by Other Municipalities

The following alternatives are HAF-safe at Tier 4 or lower, doctrinally defensible, and used in peer jurisdictions. They can be combined.

1. Registration + design standards + STR business-licence (the recommended default package). Registration regimes survive Charter and reasonableness review; design and form-based standards regulate use intensity rather than user identity. STR business licences (Calgary, Vancouver, Toronto, Saskatoon, Victoria) are HAF-neutral density levers because they constrain transient occupancy, not long-term suite supply. This package is the cleanest HAF surface among the cap-adjacent options.

2. Parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria (Red Deer Tier 4 model — without the numerical cap). Red Deer's Location Criteria — 100-metre / block / street / row proximity considerations, design and accessibility standards, parking — are applied at the development-permit stage to Discretionary applications. Adopted independently of a numerical cap, this mechanism appears to coexist with HAF compliance on the available evidence. It addresses the same neighbourhood-character concerns council often cites for caps, but at the parcel rather than the aggregate level. This is the strongest cap-adjacent option for Airdrie if a Red-Deer-style mechanism is politically required.

3. Parking minimums calibrated to documented saturation. Parking minimums survive Charter and reasonableness review where calibrated to measurable need and applied uniformly. Note that Airdrie has already reduced parking from "1 per bedroom" to "1 per suite" under B-30/2024, and CMHC has identified parking-minimum reduction as a HAF compliance commitment for many recipients. Any increase in parking minimums post-HAF would be Tier 2 risk — calibration only, not tightening.

4. STR principal-residence requirement with night cap. Rocky View County's model (Bylaw C-8640-2025, in force 2025-07-08) is the regional-consistency template most accessible to Airdrie. Vancouver, Toronto, Saskatoon, and Calgary operate similar regimes. A 180-night cap is the documented Toronto / Saskatoon level; tighter caps (90-night, BC pre-Bill-35) require Airdrie-specific listing-pattern data not currently available. Stress test residual: Rocky View has been in force under one year with no published outcome data — retain the regional-consistency argument with a one-line track-record caveat where the template is recommended.

Not recommended without further verification: lot-size and lot-width minimums (adverse-effect exposure under s. 15 / AHRA); separation distances (high Alcoholism Foundation risk where not tied to a measurable operational impact); owner-occupancy requirements (jurisdictionally tested but medium risk).

D. Role of STR Regulation

STR tools are the most accessible HAF-safe density lever available to Airdrie. STR licences and principal-residence requirements do not constrain secondary-suite long-term supply and therefore sit outside the HAF compliance surface entirely. Airdrie's current public posture on STRs is undeveloped — no dedicated bylaw is on the public site — which means the STR space is effectively a greenfield window for council to act in.

Three STR mechanisms are recommended for Airdrie:

  1. STR business licence with principal-residence requirement (Vancouver, Toronto, Calgary, Saskatoon model). Prevents conversion of suites into commercial STRs without restricting long-term suite supply. Functions as a long-term rental supply protection lever.
  2. STR prohibition in secondary suites where the suite is not the operator's principal residence. Protects the suite housing stock from STR conversion while preserving the operator's right to STR the principal residence. This is the design Stream E §11.5 identifies as the equity-adjusted baseline package. A vacancy-triggered contingency design (relaxation if Airdrie's vacancy rate rises above a defined threshold) is available to address the income-control critique for stretched owner-occupiers.
  3. Reasonable night cap (180-night Toronto / Saskatoon baseline). Calibration to Airdrie's commuter-market reality is needed; the 90-night BC pre-Bill-35 number is tighter but lacks Airdrie-specific listing-pattern data to support.

Calibration caveat for council: Airdrie's commuter-market profile means STR supply effects on the local long-term rental pool are marginal relative to Vancouver / Toronto. STR regulation is therefore best framed as community-amenity and complaint-management protection with a supplementary long-term-supply-protection function, not as a primary affordable-housing lever.

E. Strategic Considerations for Airdrie's Decision-Makers

1. HAF is the binding constraint. CMHC's Action Plan Summary lists $24.839M in total estimated eligibility; local reporting in the dossier states $12.4M received and 39% of milestones complete as of late 2025, with the next advance expected March 2026. Confirm current disbursement status before publication. Any density-controlling mechanism that lands in Tier 1 or Tier 2 of the HAF taxonomy puts the remaining advances at material risk. The Calgary letter and the Red Deer termination are the controlling precedents. Council should treat HAF compliance as a non-negotiable design constraint, not as a political variable.

2. Decision timing is live. The public consultation on the four-homes-per-lot regulation closes 2026-05-25 (Phase 2 lock date for this dossier). The next council policy window on suite density is therefore in the immediate term. The dossier is designed to inform that window directly.

3. Staff accountability for the file is publicly unclear. Landscape research did not identify a Planning Director on Airdrie's public-facing organizational chart as the file lead for either the four-units-as-of-right regulation or any prospective STR bylaw. Council should clarify staff accountability before any bylaw amendment is brought forward — both for procedural integrity and to ensure a single file owner is positioned to manage the CMHC and provincial communications burden any amendment would entail.

4. Renter consultation is a credibility precondition. Stress test residual R2 applies: this dossier was produced without renter-side consultation. Before any bylaw drafting, council should hear from renter-side stakeholders — Community Links, Airdrie Housing Limited, and Calgary-CMA newcomer service providers are the named candidates. This is an equity-credibility move that materially hardens any policy package council adopts against subsequent challenge.

5. Measurement is a credibility precondition. Stress test residual R1 applies: Airdrie has no public baseline on complaint volume, parking saturation, STR listing count, fiscal model, or rental-stock conversion. Council should establish a 12-month measurement program before or alongside any cap-family mechanism, with Phase 2 regulatory calibration following the measurement data. A no-action baseline should be included in any options analysis so council can measure consequences of inaction against each intervention.

6. Provincial dynamics may favour amendment but federal dynamics do not. Given the Premier's and Minister Nixon's public posture in defence of Red Deer, provincial approval may be more readily available for an amendment that retreats from federal supply mandates than at any earlier point. But federal approval would simultaneously become harder. The two-key PPA / CMHC structure means a council pursuing a retreat must plan for a multi-month, multi-stakeholder process with no guarantee of either approval. A cap adopted without a negotiated amendment (i.e., unilateral) is the worst outcome — federal funding at risk, no provincial cover, and exposure to repayment claims.

F. Evidence-Based Conclusion — Caps vs Alternatives

Recommended package for Airdrie (the default):

  • Adopt: Registration regime + design / form-based standards + STR business licence with principal-residence requirement + reasonable night cap (180-night baseline, calibrate to Airdrie listing data).
  • Add as a Tier 4 option, if council requires a cap-adjacent mechanism: Red-Deer-style parcel-level discretionary Location Criteria (proximity, design, accessibility, parking), applied at the development-permit stage to Discretionary applications. Do not layer a numerical neighbourhood cap on top — the cap is what creates Tier 2 HAF exposure; the parcel-level criteria can stand alone at Tier 4.
  • Establish: 12-month measurement baseline (complaint volume, parking-saturation observation, STR registration uptake, rental-stock-conversion data). Phase 2 calibration of any cap-family mechanism follows the measurement data, not the other way around.
  • Convene: Renter-side consultation (Community Links, Airdrie Housing Limited, Calgary-CMA newcomer providers) as a precondition to bylaw drafting.

Not recommended:

  • A numerical neighbourhood concentration cap adopted post-HAF without an empirical baseline (Tier 2 HAF risk + Vavilov exposure + s. 15 / AHRA adverse-effect exposure).
  • Any mechanism that constrains Airdrie's agreed four-units-as-of-right commitment (Tier 1 HAF risk — termination is the documented outcome).
  • Parking-minimum increases (Tier 2 HAF risk; CMHC has identified parking reduction as a compliance commitment).
  • Separation distances absent a documented operational-impact rationale (high Alcoholism Foundation risk).

Bottom line for council. Concentration caps on secondary suites are uncommon, conditionally defensible, and — in Airdrie's specific HAF-bound position — the highest-risk regulatory surface short of outright refusal of a mandated commitment. The available evidence supports a registration + design + STR-licence package as the recommended default, with parcel-level discretionary criteria as the cap-adjacent option if politically required. The single most defensibility-hardening step council can take is to pair any regulatory package with a 12-month measurement program and renter-side consultation, so the resulting bylaw rests on a record rather than on assertion.
